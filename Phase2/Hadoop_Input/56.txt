     <h2 class="intro big">Make Your Move</h2> 
     <h3 class="verd intro">Take a free college course, access summer research opportunities, be a campus leader and more when you transfer to UCR.</h3> 
     <p> In addition to our <strong>Transfer Admission Guarantee (TAG) program</strong> for California community college students, we offer transfer support programs for Puente Project participants, Mt. San Jacinto College students and STEM students, too. </p>

        <div class="clearfix grid_6 alpha"> 
         <h4 class="verd">UC Riverside Puente Connection</h4> 
         <p> We created Puente Connection to provide <strong>Puente Project Participants</strong>, or “Puentistas,” with more opportunities to connect with UCR and build leadership skills. </p> 
         <p> Our <a href="https://highlanderlink.ucr.edu/organization/puenteconnection">Puente Connection</a> student organization is the first of its kind in the UC system and provides continual support to Puentistas who enroll at UCR. </p> 
         <p> Puente Connection mentors are former community college Puentistas that have transferred to UCR. They provide a student perspective on topics such as the application process, financial aid, professors, student life and more. </p> 
        </div> 
        <div class="grid_6 omega"> 
         <br />

        <div class="grid_12"> 
         <h4 class="verd" style="color: #d56642;">Attend the 7th Annual UCR Puente Leadership Conference <br /> June 23-July 3, 2014</h4> 
         <p> <img alt="Future Leaders" src="/Images/TransferPrograms/FutureLeaders.jpg" /> </p> 
         <p> <strong>Future Leaders:</strong> Apply for the UCR Puente Leadership Conference! It’s a FREE 10-day residential program at UCR that brings together Puente Project participants from community colleges throughout California. </p> 
         <p> 60 students will be selected to engage in activities that promote academics, leadership and community involvement. Participants will learn how to run a successful campaign, discuss issues affecting students in higher education and exchange ideas with Puentistas from across the state! </p> 
         <p> <a href="https://docs.google.com/forms/d/1r1UD1i-jD8wN7MADjSbSZUGHg0kuXQYagMuXWmCqdu0/viewform">APPLY NOW for the 2014 UCR Puente Leadership Conference.</a> <br /> <br /> <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/16196">Read about the 2013 Puente Leadership conference in UCR Today.</a> </p> 
         <p> <strong>For more information, please contact:</strong><br /> <strong>Karolia Macias</strong><br /> Admissions Counserlor<br /> <a href="mailto:karolia.macias@ucr.edu">karolia.macias@ucr.edu</a><br /> </p>

      <h2>Using Holograms to Improve Electronic Devices</h2> 
      <p class="deck">Researchers have demonstrated a holographic memory device that could improve storage capacity and processing capabilities in electronics</p> 
      <p class="byline">By <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/author/snealon" title="View all posts by Sean Nealon" rel="author">Sean Nealon</a> on <em>February 19, 2014</em></p>

      <div class="figure_main"> 
       <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/20613/attachment/007"><img width="603" height="400" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/007-603x400.jpg" class="attachment-feature" alt="A close up shot of the holographic memory device" /></a>
       <p>A prototype of the holographic memory device built in the lab of Alexander Khitun.</p> 
      </div> 
      <p>RIVERSIDE, Calif. (<a href="http://www.ucr.edu">www.ucr.edu</a>) — A team of researchers from the University of California, Riverside <a href="http://www.engr.ucr.edu/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.engr.ucr.edu/']);">Bourns College of Engineering</a> and Russian Academy of Science have demonstrated a new type of holographic memory device that could provide unprecedented data storage capacity and data processing capabilities in electronic devices.</p> 
      <p>The new type of memory device uses spin waves – a collective oscillation of spins in magnetic materials – instead of the optical beams. Spin waves are advantageous because spin wave devices are compatible with the conventional electronic devices and may operate at a much shorter wavelength than optical devices, allowing for smaller electronic devices that have greater storage capacity.</p> 
      <p>Experimental results obtained by the team show it is feasible to apply holographic techniques developed in optics to magnetic structures to create a magnonic holographic memory device. The research combines the advantages of the magnetic data storage with the wave-based information transfer.</p> 
      <div id="attachment_20618" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 356px">
       <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/20613/034-2" rel="attachment wp-att-20618"><img class="size-medium wp-image-20618" alt="headshot of Alexander Khitun" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/0341-356x236.jpg" width="356" height="236" /></a>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">Alexander Khitun, a research professor at UC Riverside.</p>
      </div> 
      <p>“The results open a new field of research, which may have tremendous impact on the development of new logic and memory devices,” said <a href="http://www.mse.ucr.edu/LecturersandStaffResearchers.html" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.mse.ucr.edu/LecturersandStaffResearchers.html']);">Alexander Khitun</a>, the lead researcher, who is a research professor at UC Riverside.</p> 
      <p>A paper, “Magnonic Holographic Memory,” that describes the finding has been submitted for publication in the journal Applied Physics Letters. An advance copy of the paper can be accessed at: <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1401.5133" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://arxiv.org/abs/1401.5133']);">http://arxiv.org/abs/1401.5133</a></p> 
      <p>There are three co-authors of the paper: Frederick Gertz, a graduate student who works with Khitun at UC Riverside, and A. Kozhevnikov and Y. Filimonov, both of the Russian Academy of Sciences.</p> 
      <p>Holography is a technique based on the wave nature of light which allows the use of wave interference between the object beam and the coherent background. It is commonly associated with images being made from light, such as on driver’s licenses or paper currency. However, this is only a narrow field of holography.</p> 
      <p>The first holograms were designed in the last 1940s for use with electron microscopes.&nbsp; A decade later, with the advent of the laser, optical holographic images were popularized. Since, other fields have significantly advanced by using wave interference to produce holograms, including acoustic holograms used in seismic applications and microwave holography used in radar systems.</p> 
      <p>Holography has been also recognized as a future data storing technology with unprecedented data storage capacity and ability to write and read a large number of data in a highly parallel manner.</p> 
      <div id="attachment_20614" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 271px">
       <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/20613/khitun-diagram" rel="attachment wp-att-20614"><img class="size-medium wp-image-20614" alt="Images of holograms " src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/02/khitun-diagram-271x356.jpg" width="271" height="356" /></a>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">A set of three holograms obtained for the three configurations of the top micro-magnets as illustrated by the schematics.</p>
      </div> 
      <p>Khitun has been working for more than nine years to develop logic device exploiting spin waves. Most of his initial research was focused on the development of spin wave-based logic circuits similar to the ones currently used in the computers.</p> 
      <p>A critical moment occurred last year when he decided the device didn’t need to replace the computer’s electronic circuits. Instead, the device would complement the circuits, or help them accomplish certain tasks, such as image recognition, speech recognition and data processing.</p> 
      <p>The experiments outlined in the paper were conducted using a 2-bit magnonic holographic memory prototype device. A pair of magnets, which represent the memory elements, were aligned in different positions on the magnetic waveguides.</p> 
      <p>Spin waves propagating through the waveguides are affected by the magnetic field produced by the magnets. &nbsp;When spin waves interference was applied in the experiments, a clear picture was produced and the researchers could recognize the magnetic states of the magnets. All experiments were done at room temperature.</p> 
      <p>The research was supported in part by: <a href="http://fame-nano.org/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://fame-nano.org/']);">The Center for Function Accelerated nanoMaterial Engineering (FAME)</a>, which is funded with $35 million from the Semiconductor Research Corporation (SRC), a consortium of semiconductor industry companies; the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency; and the National Science Foundation under the NEB2020 Grant ECCS-1124714.</p>
