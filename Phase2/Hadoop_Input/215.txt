     <h3>UCR Campus Computer Labs</h3> 
     <p>SCS maintains seven public computer labs featuring approximately 168 computers available for academic use by all UCR students, with open hours of approximately 160 hours per week. Whether you prefer to work on a PC or a Macintosh, just need to type a Word document or need to edit a film project in Final Cut Pro, the student computer labs are here to support you.</p> 
     <p>To login to the SCS computer labs, use your UCR Net ID and password.</p> 
     <h4>Who May Use the SCS Labs</h4> 
     <ul> 
      <li><strong>All registered UCR students</strong>.</li> 
      <li><strong>UCR Faculty</strong> - Faculty instructing a course may <a href="/scs/reslabreq.html">reserve our facilities for instructional use</a> or <a href="/scs/ressoftreq.html">request to have software installed</a> on our machines. However, they will need to obtain a Guest Account (please email the <a href="mailto:phyllis.franco@ucr.edu">SCS manager</a>).</li> 
      <li><strong>UCR Alumni Association Members </strong> may use the SCS computing facilities in Sproul Hall 2225, Olmsted Hall 1316, and Watkins Hall 2101, 2111, and 2117 if they obtain an Alumni Account. To obtain an account, please use one of our kiosks.</li> 
      <li><strong>Concurrently Enrolled Extension Students </strong> are eligible to use the SCS computing facilities in Sproul Hall 2225, Olmsted Hall 1316, and Watkins Hall 2101, 2111, and 2117 once they obtain an Extension Account. To obtain an account, please use one of our kiosks .</li> 
      <li><strong>Students, faculty, and staff from other UC campuses</strong> are generally NOT permitted to use the facilities. However, for access to the Internet and email, they may use the computers in the Libraries.</li> 
     </ul>

      <h4>Introduction</h4> 
      <p>UCR offers secure remote access to UCR resources using Virtual Private Network technology. You can think of a Virtual Private Network (VPN) as a secure data tunnel between your computer (either at home or while traveling) and the UCR and other resource networks. UCR offers three different types of VPN service: Cisco's AnyConnect Client VPN, Smart-Tunnel VPN, and WebVPN. The three services are aimed at different usages, and offer different primary benefits. VPN Services are available to students, staff, and faculty in the UCR community.</p> 
      <h4>AnyConnect VPN</h4> 
      <p>UCR is now offering Cisco's AnyConnect client VPN.&nbsp; This is the preferred method of accessing UCR resources remotely. AnyConnect establishes a secure tunnel between the user's computer and the campus network with no configuration required by the user. This connection gives access to university computing resources, including those not available through a browser, from off-campus. AnyConnect has the advantage of being able to be utilized in all access situations (such as non web-based resources). For more information on installing the AnyConnect client VPN software please visit the &quot;More information on using AnyConnect VPN&quot; page for detailed instructions or contact the help desk.</p> 
      <ul class="standout"> 
       <li><a href="/vpn/anyconnect/anyconnect.html"> More information on using AnyConnect VPN</a></li> 
       <li><a href="https://vpn.ucr.edu">Access AnyConnect</a></li> 
      </ul> 
      <h4>Smart-Tunnel</h4> 
      <p>UCR is now offering Cisco's Smart-Tunnel technology. The &quot;Smart Tunnel&quot; is an extension of the WebVPN and is available for use on PCs running Windows and Macintosh operating systems. This feature allows browsers to access websites through the WebVPN as if the PC was on campus without the need for additional software. This does however, require Java or ActiveX to be installed and running. Unlike using the browser located inside the WebVPN portal, this feature requires that a new tab or window be opened on the browser to be utilized. For more information on using the Smart-Tunnel technology, please visit the &quot;More information on using Smart-Tunnel&quot; page for detailed instructions or contact the help desk.</p> 
      <ul class="standout"> 
       <li><a href="/vpn/smart-tunnel.html">More information on using Smart-Tunnel</a></li> 
       <li><a href="https://vpn.ucr.edu">Access Smart-Tunnel</a></li> 
      </ul> 
      <h4>WebVPN <em>(This service will be removed in Spring of 2013)</em></h4> 
      <p>Computing &amp; Communications and the UCR Libraries have collaborated to offer WebVPN as a lightweight access solution for users off-campus.&nbsp;WebVPN allows student, faculty, and staff users to access web-based resources through a secure browser session, as if they were on campus. Importantly, WebVPN does not require the installation of any client software. This makes it ideal for use on public computers, or for users who cannot install software on the computer at hand. Because of some limitations, Computing &amp; Communications recommends WebVPN only in situations where AnyConnect is not possible. Please note as well, that WebVPN does not allow access to non-web-based resources. For more information on using WebVPN, please visit the &quot;More information on using WebVPN&quot; page for detailed instructions or contact the help desk.</p> 
      <ul class="standout"> 
       <li><a href="/vpn/webvpn.html">More information on using WebVPN</a></li> 
       <li><a href="https://vpn.ucr.edu">Access WebVPN</a></li> 
       <li><a href="http://library.ucr.edu/?view=help/remoteaccess/vpn_faq.html">UCR Libraries list of web-based resources that do not work with WebVPN </a></li> 
      </ul>

     <h3><img class="img_L" title="Kelsey Schall" src="/images/kelsey_schall_ucr.jpg" alt="Kelsey Schall" width="150" height="182" />Kelsey Schall</h3> 
     <p>Graduate Student Researcher<br />(951) 827-4360<br />kelsey.schall@ucr.edu</p> 
     <p>Kelsey Schall started as a graduate student in the Hoddle lab in September 2013 and is currently working on her PhD in Entomology. Earlier in the year she completed her Bachelor’s degree as a Regent’s Scholar at the University of California Santa Barbara. As an undergraduate researcher, she studied parasitic interactions in marine life and worked independently on a survey of insect diversity and density at the Carpinteria Salt Marsh Reserve. Her background helped fashion her current interest in parasitic wasp behavior and development, and she hopes to complement integrated pest management strategies with her research. <br />Over the summer of 2013, Kelsey received funding from UCR’s Grad Edge program to study interactions between the invasive Argentine ant (<em>Linepithema humile</em>) and <em>Tamarixia radiata</em>, an ectoparasitoid of the Asian Citrus Psyllid (<em>Diaphorina citri</em>). She completed a project that examined the effect of Argentine ant density in citrus trees on successful parasitism of <em>D. citri</em> by <em>T. radiata</em>. She also looked at the impact of a drop in Argentine ant density on host switching from tending ACP to tending more “attractive” citrus honeydew producers such as brown scale and mealybug. Her current research focuses on host competition between two <em>D. citri</em> parasitoids, the aforementioned <em>T. radiata</em> and <em>Diaporencyrtus aligarhensis</em>. She hopes to determine if this interaction is a contributing factor to the lack of establishment of <em>D. aligarhensis</em> in Florida.</p> 
     <h3>Awards</h3>

      <li>Grad Edge summer program, 2013</li>

     </ul> 
     <p>&nbsp;</p> 
     <h3>Current Projects</h3> 
     <p>&nbsp;</p> 
     <h3 style="margin-left: 30px;"><a href="http://cisr.ucr.edu/asian_citrus_psyllid.html"><img id="placeholder3" class="img_L" title="Asian Citrus Psyllid" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/asian_citrus_psyllid_thumb.jpg" alt="Asian Citrus Psyllid" width="132" height="82" /></a><a href="http://cisr.ucr.edu/asian_citrus_psyllid.html">Asian Citrus Psyllid&nbsp;<span>(</span><em>Diaphorina citri</em><span>)</span></a></h3> 
     <p style="margin-left: 30px;"><span>Asian citrus psyllid (ACP) is an efficient vector of the bacterial citrus disease&nbsp;</span><a href="http://cisr.ucr.edu/citrus_greening.html">huanglongbing (HLB)</a><span>, previously called citrus greening disease, which is one of the most destructive diseases of citrus worldwide.</span></p> 
     <p>&nbsp;</p> 
     <h3 style="margin-left: 30px;"><img id="placeholder3" class="img_L" title="Argentine Ant" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/argentine_ant_thumb.jpg" alt="Argentine Ant" width="132" height="86" /><a href="http://cisr.ucr.edu/argentine_ant.html">Argentine Ant&nbsp;<span>(</span><em>Linepithema humile</em></a>)</h3> 
     <p style="margin-left: 30px;"><span>The Argentine ant is native to Northern Argentina and it is a globally distributed invasive pest in urban, natural, and agricultural habitats. Due to unusually low levels of intraspecific aggression the Argentine ant can establish extremely large colonies.</span></p>

     <h3>UCR: Your Passport to the World's Emerging Markets</h3> 
     <p>Welcome to The Global Programs offered exclusively through UCR’s School of Business Administration.&nbsp; We are eager to get you on board to see the world. This website provides important information on the Program’s mission, the country portfolio and the important deadlines.</p> 
     <p><strong>The mission of the Global Programs</strong> is to open opportunities for students of all majors – both undergraduate and graduate – to strengthen their resumes with international exposure by seeing first-hand the most important, dynamic and emerging markets in the world.</p> 
     <h3>The Global Programs: An Asset For Your Future</h3> 
     <p><span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em;"><img class="img_R" style="margin: 10px;" src="/images/global_china4.jpg" alt="China" width="250" height="161" />We designed the Program with one overarching aim – to help you add competitive value to your resume.&nbsp; Remember, how you position yourself in the marketplace will be the difference between the job you want, the income that matches your talent and your long-term competitiveness.&nbsp; Global exposure is one of the best ways to show your future employer that you are a serious candidate and that you value diversity of culture and ideas.&nbsp;</span></p> 
     <p>All students who have maximized their Global Program investments have shared their stories about <strong>the job interview</strong> – their trip overseas was one of the winning factors of landing the job.&nbsp;</p> 
     <p><span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em;">For example, a student from our first Oxford program shared his story of his interview for the USC master’s in accountancy program.&nbsp; One of the highlights of the interview was the discussion of his exposure to world-class lectures and site visits while in residence at Oxford.&nbsp; This UCR and USC alum is now working for one of the Big Four accounting firms in Los Angeles.&nbsp;</span></p> 
     <p><span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em;"><img class="img_L" style="margin: 10px;" src="/images/global_oxford4.png" alt="Oxford" width="350" height="177" />Another recent example comes from a student who invested in all of the trips over her time as a marketing major at the School of Business Administration.&nbsp; Her exposure to Oxford, India and China was a major differentiator in landing her a job after graduation working at Walmart’s corporate headquarters.&nbsp; She is well-positioned to understand the challenges and opportunities impacting the world’s largest retail firm.</span></p> 
     <p>Recently, one of our students who invested in both the China and Oxford programs was able to land a summer internship at a Los Angeles-based Chinese solar company working directly with the CEO.&nbsp; Having Fudan University (one of China’s premiere universities) on his resume enabled him to speak with authority about China’s manufacturing and distribution into the American market while also giving him the skillset to negotiate a higher salary due to his expertise in international management culture.</p> 
     <p><img class="img_R" style="margin: 10px;" src="/images/global_india1.jpeg" alt="India" width="250" />Several students outside of the School of Business Administration join the Program as well.&nbsp; For example, students from psychology, sociology, anthropology and political science now have global experience through investing in The Global Programs.&nbsp; Graduate students from across campus have also made the investment.&nbsp; Recently, one of UCR’s doctoral students in neuroscience joined the Oxford program whose entrepreneurship theme exposed her to the possibilities of taking her talents in the sciences and one day bring them to the marketplace.</p> 
     <p>Since the Program began with our first trip to Oxford in 2010, over 100 students have invested in The Global Programs and this coming year we will be adding two new markets to our portfolio – Russia and Japan.&nbsp; As you explore the various global opportunities within the website, ask the following questions:&nbsp;</p> 
     <p><strong>How can I make my resume more competitive?&nbsp; </strong><br /> <strong>Does my resume have global exposure?<br /></strong> <strong>Does my resume differentiate from the rest of the pile?</strong></p> 
     <p>We look forward to getting you on board to see the world and increasing your competitiveness in the marketplace.</p> 
     <p style="text-align: left;" align="center"><strong>Welcome to the Global Program!</strong></p> 
     <h4 class="section2"><em>About Your Professor, Dr. Sean Jasso</em></h4> 
     <p><em><img class="img_L" style="margin: 5px;" src="/images/global_jasso.jpeg" alt="Sean Jasso" width="85" height="109" />Dr. Sean Jasso has served as a lecturer in management at UCR for over ten years.&nbsp; His main teaching and research disciplines include strategic management, marketing management and corporate governance &amp; ethics.&nbsp; Dr. Jasso’s expertise also includes political economy, comparative politics, public policy and globalization.&nbsp; He designed The Global Programs in 2010 taking the first student team to University of Oxford.&nbsp; Since then, the Programs have added China, India, Russia and Japan.&nbsp; He enjoys teaching students about the world’s competitive markets and encourages students to gain as much global exposure as they can.&nbsp; Dr. Jasso’s main goal <strong>for you </strong>is to help you to strengthen your resume and The Global Program’s Mission is a great place to start.&nbsp; He looks forward to traveling with you this year. Contact him at <a href="mailto:sean.jasso@ucr.edu">sean.jasso@ucr.edu</a>.</em></p>
