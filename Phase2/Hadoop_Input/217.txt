      <h3 class="highlight">Five Important Initiatives Enhancing Security @ UCR</h3> 
      <!-- com.omniupdate.div label="morecontent" group="Everyone" button="657" -->
      <!-- com.omniupdate.editor csspath="/z-omniupdate/css/style.css" cssmenu="/z-omniupdate/css/style.txt" width="484" -->
      <p>UCR’s electronic systems, communications resources, and cyber infrastructures are fundamental tools used to facilitate campus research, instruction, public service, and administrative processes. To protect these valuable resources, the campus has acted on five important initiatives aimed at enhancing cyber security.&nbsp; An overview of these efforts is as follows:</p> 
      <ul class="standout"> 
       <li>UCR has adopted a suite of Minimum Standards for all devices connecting to the campus network. These standards will help ensure the campus electronic resources are available, reliable, and secure.</li> 
       <li>UCR provides anti-virus software available to all faculty, students, and staff at no cost.</li> 
       <li>UCR also provides anti-spyware software available to all faculty, students, and staff at no cost.</li> 
       <li>UCR departments have access to a web based tool for acquiring computers and laptops at extremely competitive prices. Access to this new tool (eBuy PC+) will help ensure campus computers are upgraded on a regular basis and are thus capable of running secure operating systems as well as anti-virus and anti-spyware software.</li> 
       <li>Finally, UCR has created and deployed an on-line Security Tutorial providing background on important security related issues as well as practical techniques for securing and protecting campus electronic resources, especially personal computers.</li> 
      </ul>

      <h3 class="section">The Edward A. Dickson Emeritus/a Professorship</h3> 
      <p>The Edward A. Dickson Emeritus/a Professorship Award at the Riverside campus will be awarded beginning with the 2008-2009 academic year and annually thereafter, to one or more emeritus professors for teaching, research, or public service activities that are in accordance with the intent of the donor. The awardee(s) shall be known as the Edward A. Dickson Emeritus/a Professor for the duration of the award. The award also conveys up to $6,000 which may be used for salary, travel or research support</p> 
      <br />

        <td><img class="img_L" style="margin-top: 22px; margin-bottom: 22px;" title="Ruth Amrich" src="/images/ruth_amrich_ucr.jpg" alt="Ruth Amrich" width="150" height="182" /></td> 
        <td valign="top"> <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1.5em; line-height: 1.333em; font-weight: lighter; letter-spacing: 0.03em; color: #2668b2; padding: 0px;">Ruth Amrich<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Lab Assistant II<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Phone: 951-827-4360</span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><a href="mailto:ruth.amrich@ucr.edu">ruth.amrich@ucr.edu</a></span></p> <p>Ruth attained her degree in Biology from California State University, San Bernardino in 1996. There she worked with Dr. Alexander Sokoloff with genetically mutated <em>Tribolium</em> species as a lab assistant maintaining colonies. She joined the Hoddle lab in 2001.</p> <p>Through the years, Ruth has been involved with multiple projects with Dr. Hoddle including avocado pests: the avocado thrips <em>Scirtothrips perseae</em> (and its biocontrol agent <em>Franklinothrips orizabensis</em>) and the persea mite, <em>Oligonychus perseae</em> (and predator mites that attack this pest). Ruth has helped with projects on <em>Levuana iridescens</em>, and the bean thrips <em>Caliothrips fasciatus</em> (interbreeding capabilities between different populations, and the effects of cold storage on survival and reproduction).</p> <p>With Dr. Nic Irvin, she helped with research on transgenic <em>Aedes aegypti</em>, and various natural enemies, of <em>Homalodisca vitripennis</em> (e.g., <em>Gonatocerus triguttatus</em>, <em>Gonatocerus fasciatus</em>, and <em>Gonatocerus ashmeadi</em>). With the WSARE project in the Temecula wine country Ruth helped with ELISA work.</p> <p>Ruth has assisted graduate students, post-docs, doctoral candidates, and collaborators with their research projects, too. These have included: the effects of <em>Xylella fastidiosa</em> on Australian native plants; day-degree and life table studies on <em>G. ashmeadi</em> and <em>H. vitripennis</em>; oviposition studies of <em>Graphocephala atropunctata</em> on wild grapes; mulch studies in avocados for pest control; and pollination studies on Hass avocados.</p> <p>She has tackled microlepidoptera preservation, DNA extraction of <em>Thysanoptera</em> and slide mounting of the resulting specimens, assisting with Dr. Pandey’s quarantine studies on Asian citrus pysillid (<em>Diaphorina citri</em> Kuwayama), <em>Tamarixia radiata</em>, and California native psyllids; assisting Vanessa Lopez with her gold-spotted oak borer (<em>Agrilus auroguttatus</em>) research; with Ricky Lara on continuing binomial sampling of avocado mites and time series analysis; and with various cooperative agencies for the ACP and emerald ash borer(<em>Agrilus planipennis</em>) projects. From her involvement in varied projects, Ruth has been listed as a second [link to Ricky's] and third [link to Paul's] author on two recent publications.</p> <p>Currently, Ruth's time is split between monitoring <em>D.citri</em> and <em>T.radiata</em> life stages on mixed citrus and curry of residential properties around urban Los Angeles and Riverside counties, monitoring the effects of ant activities on parasitism rates, assisting Allison with rearing quarantined colonies of <em>T.radiata</em>, materials manager for the lab, as well as the resident seamstress. In addition, she is also the Department of Entomology Laboratory auditor, Ambassador to UCR Staff Assembly, an Affirmative Action and Compliance Liaison for the Hoddle Lab to Human Resources, and the Lab Khaleesi.</p> </td>

        <td> <h3 style="margin-top: 1em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1.5em; line-height: 1.333em; font-weight: lighter; letter-spacing: 0.03em; color: #2668b2; padding: 0px;">Mike Lewis<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Lab Assistant III<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Phone: 951-827-4360</span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: x-small;"><span style="font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px;"><strong style="font-weight: bold; padding: 0px; margin: 0px;"><a href="mailto: michael.lewis@ucr.edu;">michael.lewis@ucr.edu</a></strong></span></span></h3> <p style="margin-top: 0.5em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em; padding: 0px;">Mike started with the Hoddle lab 2004, and helps with a number of support roles within the lab. In addition&nbsp;to managing the lab computer and tech network, he manages the Center for Invasive Species Research (CISR) website which has over 15,000 unique visitors each month. He also contributes blog posts and photographs of the invasive species listed on the CISR site, as well as other graphic materials.</p> <p style="margin-top: 0.5em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em; padding: 0px;">Mike provides support for many of the research projects within the Hoddle lab. Some of the research he has been involved in includes glassy-winged sharpshooter (<em>Homalodisca vitripennis</em>), gold spotted oak borer (<em>Agrilus auroguttatus</em>), avocado thrips (<em>Scirtothrips perseae</em>), mealybugs (<em>Pseudococcidae</em>), <em>Gonatocerus ashmeadi</em>, Persea mites (<em>Oligonychus perseae</em>), Asian citrus psyllid, (<em>Diaphorina citri</em>) and <em>Tamarixia radiata</em>.</p> <p style="margin-top: 0.5em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em; padding: 0px;">A gallery of some of Mike’s photography with the Center for Invasive Species can be found <a href="http://needpictures.com/blog/category/bugscisr/" target="_blank">here</a>.</p> </td> 
       </tr> 
       <tr> 
        <td> <h3><img class="img_L" style="margin-top: 22px; margin-bottom: 22px;" title="Martin Castillo" src="/images/martin_castillo_ucr.jpg" alt="Martin Castillo" width="150" height="182" /></h3> </td> 
        <td valign="top"> <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1.5em; line-height: 1.333em; font-weight: lighter; letter-spacing: 0.03em; color: #2668b2; padding: 0px;">Martin Castillo<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Lab Assistant II<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Phone: 951-827-4360</span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><a href="mailto:martin.castillo@ucr.edu">martin.castillo@ucr.edu</a></span></p> <p>Martin Castillo received his Bachelor’s degree in 2012 from the University of California, Riverside. He first joined the Hoddle lab as an undergraduate assistant in 2010, providing field site support for Dr. Nic Irvin. His contributions included the collection of glassy-winged sharpshooter (<em>Homalodisca vitripennis</em>) from the UCR Agricultural Operations fields.</p> <p>He subsequently transitioned to working with different species of mites under the guidance of graduate student researcher Ricky Lara, which he currently continues to provide support with. Martin has worked with various species of mites over the course of this study, including <em>Oligonychus perseae</em>, <em>Euseius hibisci</em>, <em>Neoseiulus californicus</em>, <em>Stigmaeidae</em> sp., and <em>Tydeidae</em> sp. His responsibilities include mite colony rearing and maintenance on ficus and avocado leaves (<em>Ficus</em> sp., <em>Persea americana</em>), extracting pollen from ice plants (<em>Malephora crocea</em>) and cattails (<em>Typha</em> sp.), and well as recording and analyzing predatory mite behaviors using microscope-mounted video equipment. &nbsp;<span style="font-size: 1em; line-height: 1.5em;">Martin has also assisted Dr. Mark Hoddle and Ricky Lara in coordinating grower seminars, where he contributed to the development of a sequential binomial sampling plan for <em>Oligonychus perseae</em> on avocado.</span></p> <p>In the fall of 2012, Martin was promoted from an undergraduate appointment to lab staff, and joined the Asian citrus psyllid (ACP), <em>Diaphorina citri</em>, project under the mentorship of Ruth Amrich. His duties include the field observation and sampling of ACP as well as identifying life stages of specimens collected from residential citrus trees.</p> </td> 
       </tr>

        <td> <h3><img class="img_L" style="margin-top: 22px; margin-bottom: 22px;" title="Adam Olguin" src="/images/adam_olguin_ucr.jpg" alt="Adam Olguin" width="150" height="182" /></h3> </td> 
        <td valign="top"> <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1.5em; line-height: 1.333em; font-weight: lighter; letter-spacing: 0.03em; color: #2668b2; padding: 0px;">Adam Olguin<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Lab Assistant II<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Phone: 951-827-2246</span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><a href="mailto:adam.olguin@ucr.edu">adam.olguin@ucr.edu</a></span></p> <p>Adam received his Bachelor's degree in Cell Biology and Neuroscience from UCR in 2012, with a minor in Entomology. As an undergraduate, he worked in five different labs across the campus. His past projects have included work with the glassy winged sharpshooter (<em>Homolodisca vitripennis</em>) with Dr. Rick Redak; carob moth (<em>Ectomyelois ceratoniae</em>), Banks grass mite (<em>Oligonychus pratensis</em>), and bagrada bug (<em>Bagrada hilaris</em>) with Dr. Thomas Perring; red imported fire ants (<em>Solenopsis invicta</em>) with Dr. Michael Rust; persea mites (<em>Oligonychus perseae</em>) and predatory mites (<em>Neoseiulus californicus</em>) with Dr. Mark Hoddle; and the Asian citrus psyllid (ACP),&nbsp;<em>Diaphorena citri</em>,&nbsp;with Dr. Matt Daugherty.</p> <p>Adam's current work in the Daugherty lab includes assisting in monitoring and evaluating population dynamics of the invasive insect pest of citrus, the Asian citrus psyllid (ACP). He is also investigating the effects of insecticide on the population of this pest species by means of conducting Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbant Assays (ELISA) to compare concentrations of Imidacloprid with population densities. Adam also regularly collaborates with the Hoddle lab for field surveys of ACP across Los Angeles County.</p> </td> 
       </tr>

        <td valign="top"> <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-right: 0px; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-left: 0px; font-size: 1.5em; line-height: 1.333em; font-weight: lighter; letter-spacing: 0.03em; color: #2668b2; padding: 0px;">Taryn Cummings<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Lab Assistant I<br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;">Phone: 951-827-4360</span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><br style="padding: 0px; margin: 0px;" /></span><span style="color: #25558c; font-size: 13px; line-height: 18px; font-weight: bold;"><a href="mailto:taryn.cummings@ucr.edu">taryn.cummings@ucr.edu</a></span></p> <p>Taryn graduated from UCR in 2012 with her Bachelor's degree in Environmental Sciences. During her undergraduate years, she worked with Dr. Spindler in UCR's Biochemistry department researching kinase inhibitors. She also worked with Dr. Fan performing protein structure analysis.</p> <p>Currently, Taryn's appointment is divided between the Hoddle and Stouthamer labs. She is responsible for maintaining a network of 17 greenhouses between the two labs, overseeing their sanitation, pest management, irrigation, and plant nursing. She also maintains native psyllid colonies for use in non-target impact studies in UCR's Insectary and Quarantine facility. Taryn assists the Hoddle lab in performing Asian citrus psyllid (<em>Diaphorena citri</em>) fieldwork in Riverside county, as well as observations and data entry associated with the project in the Quarantine facility.</p> <p>She is currently appointed as the lab's official cupcake critic.</p> </td>
