     <div id="mission_sidebar_header"> 
      <h4 id="mission_sidebar_header_about">Mission</h4> 
     </div> 
     <div class="mission_sidebar"> 
      <p>The mission of the Institute for Integrative Genome Biology is to:</p> 
      <ul> 
       <li>Foster and support interdisciplinary collaborations among researchers on campus and in the scientific community utilizing a systems-based<sup>1</sup> approach to science</li> 
       <li>Develop novel approaches and technologies to solve complex biological problems</li> 
       <li>Train students and postdocs in&nbsp; interdisciplinary research</li> 
       <li>Explore the translational development of potentially commercial technologies and projects</li> 
      </ul> 
      <p class="definition">1 Systems based: the melding of computational approaches and technological innovations with molecular and cellular biology</p>

         <h5><a href="http://genomics.ucr.edu/news/2014-03-05_hpis.html">International Plant/Insect Interaction Symposium at UCR!</a></h5> 
         <p><strong>(March 5, 2014)</strong></p> 
         <p>In recognition of UCR's historic contributions to and current strengths in entomology, plant biology, and plant pathology, IIGB organizers were selected to host the 2nd International Hemipteran-Plant Interactions Symposium (HPIS). The three-day event will be held on June 23-25, 2014 at HUB Rm 302 and connect 200 scientists, graduate students and postdoctoral fellows from around the world.</p> 
         <h5><a href="http://genomics.ucr.edu/news/cutler021814_abc.html">ABC News Interview on Crop Research Breakthrough!</a></h5> 
         <p><strong>(March 4, 2014)</strong></p> 
         <p>ABC30 Action News Live (KFSN-TV Fresno) conducted an interview of IIGB Associate Plant Cell Biologist Sean Cutler and his major breakthrough related to the effects of quinabactin, a synthetic chemical that triggers a molecular response protecting plants from drought.</p> 
         <h5><a href="http://genomics.ucr.edu/news/2014-01-30_martinsgreen.html">Health Problems Caused by Third-hand Smoke</a></h5> 
         <p><strong>(February 4, 2014)</strong></p> 
         <p>Second-hand smoke and third-hand smoke are just as deadly as first-hand smoke, says IIGB cell biologist Manuela Martins-Green who, along with colleagues, conducted the first animal study of the effects of third-hand smoke.</p> 
         <h5><a href="http://genomics.ucr.edu/news/2014-01-28.html">A Student's Journey as a Scientist</a></h5> 
         <p><strong>(February 4, 2014)</strong></p> 
         <p>Irma Ortiz, whose parents immigrated to the US from Mexico, is the first in her family to graduate from college. Today, Irma is a Ph.D. graduate student in IIGB geneticist Linda Walling's lab, where she and her lab-mates identify and deploy gene-based strategies for insect resistance in plants.</p> 
         <h5><a href="http://genomics.ucr.edu/news/2014-01-13_dahanukar.html">Studying How Flies Detect Sweetness</a></h5> 
         <p><strong>(February 2, 2014)</strong></p> 
         <p>IIGB researchers led by assistant professor of entomology Anupama Dahanukar have performed a study that describes just how the fly's taste receptors detect sweet compounds, potentially offering tools to control insect feeding. </p>

     <h5> <em>AGSM Reads Across America</em></h5> 
     <p> <img alt="AGSM Read Across America" src="http://soba.ucr.edu/images/news/175.jpg" style="width: 322px; height: 210px; margin: 5px; float: right;" /><em>(February 28, 2014)</em></p> 
     <p class="p1"> On February 28, staff and students from UC Riverside's A. Gary Anderson Graduate School of Management (AGSM) participated in National Education Association's (NEA) <em>Read Across America Day</em>. AGSM traveled to Monroe Elementary School in Riverside, CA to read to children and teach the importance of literacy and language.</p> 
     <p class="p1"> &quot;We're so grateful for such a high turnout! It really means a lot that AGSM was able to bring so many people,&quot; says Morgan Baeza, teacher from Monroe Elementary School. In attendance from AGSM was Irene Song, Rich Reyes, Andrew Monroe, Yi Sun, Wang Yang, Keyang Huang, Connie Kuo, Michelle Hsueh, and Jung Wang. Also joining AGSM was Ramona High School,&nbsp; California Baptist University, and the California Highway Patrol. Of the experience Song says, &quot;It was so much fun to be able to come out here and make a difference to the kids. When you see their eyes light-up, you can tell that they like it too.&quot;</p> 
     <p class="p1"> <em>Read Across America Day</em> is the NEA's signature event. This year's event is the 17th anniversary since the inception of the program and the first-time that AGSM has participated.</p>

     <p>*After a quarter ends and before the next quarter begins, there is typically some &quot;in between&quot; time.&nbsp; This amount of time varies depending on the quarter.&nbsp; For example, if the Summer quarter ends August 31st, all students wishing to receive a Summer graduation date must complete the requirements by that time.&nbsp; However, Fall quarter doesn't begin until September 28th.&nbsp; Students have until the date listed in the above table, which is the last business day before the next quarter begins, to complete the requirements and avoid fees for the next quarter.&nbsp; The conferral date for the degree would be for the next quarter (Fall in this example).&nbsp; The between quarter deadline dates in the table above are in the column that the degree would be conferred.</p> 
     <h4>Additional Information</h4> 
     <h5>Registration/Filing Fee Status</h5> 
     <p>During the quarter in which you graduate, you must either be registered or on filing fee status.&nbsp; Filing fee applications can be obtained from the Graduate Academic Affairs website at <a href="/pub_forms.html">http://graduate.ucr.edu/pub_forms.html</a>.&nbsp; Filing fee is not required, but for some students this is a great option.</p> 
     <p>Students on filing fee status do not enroll in course work and pay only one-half of the Student Services Fee.&nbsp; After the application for filing fee is approved, tuition and fees are adjusted to reflect the correct payment amount (currently $162).&nbsp; Since students on filing fee status do not enroll in course work or pay miscellaneous fees, service as a TA, GSR or any other student employment title is prohibited.&nbsp; Eligibility for any student privileges or the use of University facilities (except the Library) is also restricted. Students on filing fee may not receive fellowship funds.</p> 
     <p>To be approved for filing fee status, it is expected that a draft of the dissertation/thesis has been reviewed and approved by the committee, that only minor revisions need to be made, and that no more than 12 hours of faculty time will be required.</p> 
     <h5>Summer Session</h5> 
     <p>To file during the summer months, free of charge, you must have been enrolled (or on filing fee) every quarter of the previous academic year.&nbsp; If you were not, i.e. on leave of absence or withdrawn for one or more quarters, you may either use filing fee (if you have not already) or enroll in 2 units of dissertation/thesis research (299) in the 10-week Summer Session.</p> 
     <h5>Master's Candidates</h5> 
     <p>An Application for Advancement to Candidacy must be submitted by the first day of instruction of the quarter you plan to graduate.&nbsp; The advancement form can be found at <a href="/advancement2.html">http://graduate.ucr.edu/advancement2.html</a>.&nbsp; It is acceptable if you have not completed all the courses or other requirements needed to graduate when you submit this form.</p> 
     <h5>PhD Candidates</h5> 
     <p>Once the rough draft of your dissertation has been uploaded, we will know you intend to graduate.&nbsp; You will be sent an email directing you to additional information for completing the graduation requirements.</p> 
     <h5>Final Defense</h5> 
     <p>If your department requires a final defense, most do, verification must be received from the department that the exam has been passed or waived prior to submitting the final version of the document.</p> 
     <h5>Dissertation/Thesis Submission</h5> 
     <p>Instructions regarding the format and submission of the dissertation/thesis are available at <a href="/dissertation.html">http://graduate.ucr.edu/Dissertation.html</a>.&nbsp;</p> 
     <h5>Commencement Ceremony</h5> 
     <p>The Commencement Ceremony is held once a year, at the end of the Spring quarter.&nbsp; In February or March, watch for announcements from the Graduate Division regarding eligibility to attend the ceremony.&nbsp; The main campus commencement website is <a href="http://commencement.ucr.edu">http://commencement.ucr.edu</a>.&nbsp; The Graduate Division website with commencement information specific to graduate students is <a href="/commencement.html">http://graduate.ucr.edu/commencement.html.</a></p> 
     <h5>Diplomas and Transcript</h5> 
     <p>Diplomas are not available for at least 6 months after the degree <em>conferral</em> date.&nbsp; The Registrar's office is responsible for ordering and receiving the diplomas.&nbsp; They will not mail the diploma to you unless you arrange it, and pay the postage, with their office.&nbsp;</p> 
     <p>The degree information will be posted on your official transcript 6-8 weeks after the degree <em>conferral</em> date.&nbsp; If you would like an official transcript, you can order it through GROWL or directly through the Registrar's office.</p> 
     <h5>Certificate of Completion</h5> 
     <p>The Graduate Division understands that you often need to prove your degree completion to outside entities for employment or other reasons.&nbsp; When completing requirements for a Plan I Thesis Master's or PhD, we will issue you a Certificate of Completion.&nbsp; Students completing a Plan II master's program will not receive this document.&nbsp; This certificate is signed by the Dean of the Graduate Division and is stamped with the seal of the University.&nbsp; It will list the exact date you completed the degree requirements in addition to you conferral date.&nbsp; Most students report success when using this document for a postdoc position or other employment.</p> 
     <h5>Need Help?</h5> 
     <p>Please contact Kara Oswood in the Graduate Academic Affairs section of the Graduate Division if you have any questions regarding the information on this page.</p>

     <strong>Art History with Administrative Studies MAJOR</strong>
     <br /> This major provides students with training in management and the history of art to allow pursuit of a variety of future career and education options. With this dual preparation, students are equipped to enter fields such as arts administration, advertising, art appraisal, architecture, art investment, and historic preservation. The management, public relations, information services, accounting, human resources, and finance areas of arts institutions such as museums, research institutes, historical societies, publishers, and galleries have comprehensive needs for individuals trained in good business practices. Students can also continue their education with advanced degrees in business, museum studies, or arts administration, as well as other professional and academic fields. The History of Art and Administrative Studies share an emphasis on the cultural and social institutions of human achievement, a connection that makes possible a major that enriches both art history and administrative studies. Business majors gain knowledge of the historical, political, and social bases of visual culture, while art history majors acquire the economic, political, and sociological background to become leaders in the business of arts institutions and in related fields.&nbsp;
     <br /> 
     <br /> 
     <strong>Economics with Administrative Studies MAJOR</strong>
     <br /> The Administrative Studies component of this major provides students with an overall view of business, financial accounting, and statistical and computing skills used in business today. It includes a two-course study of organizations from an anthropological, political, psychological and sociological aspect. The capstone of this component is a three-course study in a selected area of business. The areas of choice are: 
     <ul> 
      <li>Organizations</li> 
      <li>Human Resources Management/Labor Relations</li> 
      <li>Business and Society</li> 
      <li>Marketing</li> 
      <li>Managerial Accounting/Taxation</li> 
      <li>Financial Accounting</li> 
      <li>Finance</li> 
      <li>Management Information Systems</li> 
      <li>Production management</li> 
     </ul> This Economics component of this major strengthens skills learned in administrative studies coursework. Students learn the factors that determine national income, inflation, unemployment, output, growth and inequality as well as the behavior of individual decision-making units like households and firms. Economists are concerned with the role of markets, money and interest rates, the forces affecting international trade and other problems of production and distribution. Students are prepared for further study at the graduate level or to enter careers in business, government, education and law.&nbsp;
     <br /> 
     <br /> 
     <strong>History with Administrative Studies MAJOR</strong>
     <br /> The History/Administrative Studies major is designed to combine the discipline of History, with its emphasis on changes in society over time, which the study of administrative behavior, the development of public policy, and the tools of decision making.&nbsp;
     <br /> 
     <br /> The addition of an Administrative Studies component provides History majors with analytical administrative skills as well as familiarity with the theories and policies of public administration. The concepts of organizational behavior and decision making, when combined with the perspectives provided through the History major, ought to be of particular value to those planning to enter careers in business; federal, state, or local levels of public or private administration; government work or to those planning to attend a professional school of administration or to those utilizing the major in a variety of positions in the public or private sector.&nbsp;
     <br /> 
     <br /> 
     <strong>Political Science with Administrative Studies MAJOR</strong>
     <br /> This cooperative major provides students with a systematic and rigorous look at the variety of political experiences and diversity of experiences faced both in the developing and democratic world.&nbsp;
     <br /> 
     <br /> 
     <strong>Sociology with Administrative Studies MAJOR</strong>
     <br /> Those who decide to combine this major will be exposed to a broad spectrum of topics including: ethnicity, race, class, inequality, gender, social stratification, sociology of education, the city and urban problems, the environment, sociology of religion, as well as crime and deviance.&nbsp;

     <div class="figure_main">
      <img class="attachment-feature" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/061-603x400.jpg" alt="UC Riverside researchers stand on a hybrid bulldozer" width="603" height="400" />
      <br /> 
      <p><em>UC Riverside researchers, from left, Kent Johnson, Eddie O'Neil, Don Pacocha and Sam Cao stand on a hybrid bulldozer.</em></p> 
     </div> 
     <p>RIVERSIDE, Calif. (<a href="http://www.ucr.edu/">www.ucr.edu</a>) — Hybrid technology is not necessarily greener when it comes to comparing the latest hybrid and diesel construction equipment.</p> 
     <p>That’s the main <a href="http://www.arb.ca.gov/msprog/aqip/offroad_hybrid.htm">finding of a first-of-its-kind study</a> of hybrid diesel construction equipment by researchers at the University of California, Riverside’s <a href="/">Bourns College of Engineering Center for Environmental Research and Technology (CE-CERT)</a>.</p> 
     <p>Manufacturers reported the hybrid dozers and excavators reduced fuel consumption and greenhouse gas emissions by 20 percent and cut smog-forming oxides of nitrogen emissions (NOx) by 30 percent. But, UC Riverside researchers found fuel savings of between 7 percent and 28 percent and NOx emission <em>increases</em> of up to 21 percent when compared to conventional diesel equipment.</p> 
     <p>The increase in NOx emissions illustrates the difficulty in tuning engines to simultaneously reduce both NOx and greenhouse gas emissions. Most modern diesel engines are tuned to balance the need for both NOx and greenhouse gas emission reductions, and utilize sophisticated after-treatment systems to minimize NOx before it leaves the tailpipe, said <a href="/personnel/kjohnson/index.html">Kent Johnson</a>, an assistant research engineer at CE-CERT and the principal investigator on the project.</p> 
     <p>“The reason we see increased NOx in this first generation technology is simple – getting the best fuel economy is going to sell the equipment,” said Johnson, who at CE-CERT has helped pioneer the use of portable emission measurement systems to test everything from off-road equipment to trucks, planes, trains and boats while they are being used.</p> 
     <p>He did add, however, that in the study the hybrid diesel construction equipment was compared to new conventional diesel equipment, which emits fewer harmful emissions than the older diesel equipment commonly used today.</p> 
     <p>In fact some of these newer units emit up to 90 percent less emissions compared to older units, thus the benefit of replacing old units with hybrids is a great benefit to the environment. Both hybrid diesel construction equipment and new conventional diesel construction equipment are much cleaner than old diesel equipment. The drawback is that the hybrids cost 20 percent more than new conventional diesel.</p> 
     <p>Other UC Riverside studies of today’s first generation heavy-duty hybrid vehicles and equipment have found varying results relating to emissions and fuel economy. Hybrid marine vessels performed well, while on-road heavy duty hybrids, such as tractor trailers, had mixed results.</p> 
     <p>“It all comes down to testing when the equipment is being used,” Johnson said. “That is even more important with off-road equipment, such as the construction equipment we just tested, because applications are so widely different.”</p> 
     <p>The hybrid diesel construction equipment research was conducted after CE-CERT received a <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/213">$2 million contract in 2011</a> from the <a href="http://www.arb.ca.gov/homepage.htm">California Air Resources Board</a> to evaluate two models of commercialized hybrid construction equipment – the hybrid Caterpillar D7E bulldozer and hybrid Komatsu HB215LC-1 excavator.</p> 
     <p>The research complements efforts by the California Air Resources Board to expand the demonstration and deployment of hybrid and zero-emission technology to help meet the goals of AB 32, a 2006 state law that requires greenhouse gas emissions be reduced to 1990 levels by 2020. California Governor’s Executive Order S-#-05 also targets an 80 percent reduction by 2050.</p> 
     <p>Significant deployment of next generation hybrid and zero-emission technologies is also critical for California to meet the health-based federal eight-hour ozone standard. Researchers hope the findings in this first-of-its-kind study will help drive technology improvements to hybrids that lead to even cleaner construction equipment.</p> 
     <p>The $2 million was used for two purposes: as an incentive voucher to get public agencies and companies to purchase hybrid construction equipment and to fund the emissions and fuel use research on the equipment.</p> 
     <div id="attachment_18508" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 356px;">
      <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/18506/attachment/039" rel="attachment wp-att-18508"><img class="size-medium wp-image-18508" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/039-356x236.jpg" alt="hybrid bulldozer pushing trash at landfill" width="356" height="236" /></a> 
      <p class="wp-caption-text">A hybrid bulldozer equipped with testing equipment at the Waste Management El Sobrante Landfill in Corona.</p> 
     </div> 
     <p>The researchers facilitated the deployment of 10 hybrid Caterpillar bulldozers and six hybrid Komatsu excavators to six sites in four California counties: Riverside, San Diego, Orange and Sacramento counties.</p> 
     <p>The researchers used time lapse cameras, global positioning systems, engine control units and portable emission testing equipment during more than 2,000 hours of testing on both types of vehicles. They also designed a series of standardized tests, ranging from river bed clearing to trenching, for each vehicle.</p> 
     <p>The hybrid bulldozer was tested under six conditions, each with a different distance and load amount.</p> 
     <p>Fuel savings ranged from 7 percent to 28 percent. In general, the shorter the distance and lighter the load, the more fuel saved. The use-weighted average was a 14 percent fuel savings.</p> 
     <p>Tests for emissions of nitrogen oxide (NOx), a significant contributor to air pollution, showed a range from an increase of 21 percent to a decrease of 2 percent. The use-weighted average was a 13 percent increase in NOx.</p> 
     <p>The hybrid excavator was also tested under six scenarios, ranging from demolition, which usually means longer swings of the arms, to general construction work, which consists of more trenching and backfilling.</p> 
     <p>The fuel use ranged from a 28 percent savings to an increase of 1 percent. In general, more fuel was saved during the demolition test cycles. The use-weighted average was a 16 percent fuel savings.</p> 
     <p>For emissions, the researchers tested for NOx, particulate matter and several other pollutants. For NOx there was a range from an 18 percent reduction to an 11 percent increase, with an average of a 1 percent increase. For particulate matter, there was increase between 6 percent and 36 percent, with a use-weighted average of a 27 percent increase.</p> 
     <p>Johnson believes this study could help engine manufacturers consider their designs not only from a fuel economy and certification level, but also from an in-use basis. Researchers expect as the technology continues to mature that emissions performance will improve, much as it has with hybrid cars and light trucks.</p> 
     <p>In addition to Johnson, the team of UC Riverside researchers included: Tanfeng (Sam) Cao, a graduate student; Robert L. Russell, an assistant research engineer; and George Scora, an assistant development engineer. Andrew Burnette, an environmental consultant, also was part of the team.</p>
