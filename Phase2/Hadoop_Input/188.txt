     <h5>As part of the University of California, Riverside, CE-CERT is committed to furthering education and research for the next generation of engineers.&nbsp; On October 8<sup>th</sup>, 2013 CE-CERT hosted the 2<sup>nd</sup> annual Science and Technology Education Partnership (STEP) Conference for high schools throughout the Riverside County District.&nbsp; 340 students were able to engage in a more hands-on experience focusing on either Atmospheric Transportation Engineering or Sustainable Energy Engineering. &nbsp;</h5> 
     <h5><strong>The tour focusing on atmospheric and transportation were categorized into three different stations: Atmospheric Processes Laboratory, Transportation System Research, and Emission &amp; Fuels Research.&nbsp;</strong></h5> 
     <p><strong>Atmospheric Processes Laboratory:</strong></p> 
     <p><strong><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic1.jpg" alt="APL" width="400" height="267" /></strong></p> 
     <p>Graduate students Carlos Espinoza and Derek Price showed how minute impurities in air <em>(they used water because it’s a more visual element) </em>can have enormous impacts and consequences on the environment. Fiji water was used which is not pure H20 (chemical formula) it contains some natural dissolved solids/sals, such as magnesium and calcium. The graduate students displayed that “freezing is like a puzzle, in order to see the whole picture; we must begin with two pieces that connect in the correct way. The impurities impeded that from occurring and thus did not freeze even at temperatures below the natural freezing point. When (the students) tapped the bottle the puzzle pieces or ice nuclei found the correct orientation to being forming a crystal solid. “</p> 
     <p><strong>Transportation System Research Laboratory:</strong></p> 
     <p><strong><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic2.jpg" alt="TSR -1" width="400" height="267" /></strong></p> 
     <p>Graduate students Jill Luo and Haiyu Zhang <em>(pictured below)</em> presented two research demonstrations: an Eco-driving simulator and real-time lidar imaging. The eco-driving simulator models heavy duty trucks driving on various road conditions, therefore, the students were able to experience hands-on driving scenarios observing the output of a vehicle’s speed and fuel consumption. At the end of the trial, the simulator would give the driver an eco-score, the higher the score, the more environmentally friendly the trip was. The lidar imaging is spinning at 15 times per second and is used in autonomous vehicles such as Google self-driving car. Students were able to observe the lidar movie on a TV screen and the images were projected in color to show distance.</p> 
     <p><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic3.jpg" alt="TSR - 2" width="400" height="267" /></p> 
     <p><strong style="color: #2668b2; font-size: 1.25em; line-height: 1.2em;">The tours focusing on Sustainable Energy were categorized into three stations: Thermochemical Conversion Processes, Aqueous Biomass Processing, and Solar Energy and Global Battery systems.</strong></p> 
     <p><strong>Emissions and Fuels Research Laboratory:</strong></p> 
     <p><strong><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic4.jpg" alt="EFR" width="400" height="267" /></strong></p> 
     <p>Graduate student Daniel Short simulated how a Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF) works. He used a lighter at the inlet of both Engine Exhaust Particle Sizer (EEPS) and DMM (PM Mass) and both instruments will show a strong signal of particles. Afterwards, he placed a HEPA filter in the front on the inlet of the instruments and used the lighter again, and this time the instruments were unresponsive because the HEPA filter is collecting the particles.</p> 
     <p><strong>Thermochemical Conversion Processes:</strong></p> 
     <p><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic5.jpg" alt="Thermo" width="400" height="267" /><br clear="ALL" /> This lab uses high pressure and heat to break down carbonaceous materials, such as coal or waste, into methane. The methane can then be converted into diesel fuel. Laboratory Assistant Sean Franco focused his efforts in showing the students patented Steam Hydrogasification Reaction, which uses biomass to produce alternative fuels or electricity. The reactor heats up to almost 1400 degrees and this high heats burns the organic material, turning it into carbon dioxide, methane and carbon monoxide. Sean Franco’s lab will eventually convert waste into energy so it’s now a resource it would be good for the environment.</p> 
     <p><strong>Aqueous Biomass Processing:</strong></p> 
     <p><strong><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic6.jpg" alt="Aqueous" width="400" height="267" /></strong></p> 
     <p>Graduate student Charles Cai and undergraduate student Phil Mui demonstrated<span>&nbsp;to inspire the young students to consider environmental research in the field of biofuels production from non-food biomass. They showcased a world first remote controlled vehicle powered by bioethanol in an interactive test drive and explained the steps involved to process raw plant waste and convert their sugars into ethanol. They then introduced the idea of chemical separation processes that need to be designed in order to recover and purify final products in a biorefinery plant.</span></p> 
     <p><strong>Solar Energy and Global Battery:</strong></p> 
     <p><img style="display: block; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;" src="/photos/steppic7.jpg" alt="Solar" width="400" height="267" /></p> 
     <p>Graduate students Ian Miller and Taehoon Lim along side undergraduate Darren Kwee from the Solar Energy lab hosted a hands-on activity where attendees had the opportunity to create dye-sensitized solar cells utilizing chemical compounds found in berries.&nbsp; Attendees then had the opportunity to see electricity being generated from created dye-sensitized solar cells.</p> 
     <p>CE-CERT would like to thank all the volunteers that were involved with helping this 2nd STEP Conference a success! Please visit for more details:&nbsp;<a title="STEP Conference" href="http://myscienceeducation.com/html/conferenceinfo.htm">http://myscienceeducation.com/html/conferenceinfo.htm</a>. Please stay tuned for next year's STEP Conference being held in October, 2014.&nbsp;</p>
