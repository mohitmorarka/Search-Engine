   <font face="Helvetica, Arial"><br /> <p>The <strong>Citrus Clonal Protection Program</strong> has its roots in the 1933 original discovery of the virus nature of the citrus psorosis disease by Dr. H. S. Fawcett of the Citrus Experiment Station at Riverside. That discovery triggered the establishment of the Psorosis Freedom Program in 1937. In 1957, the Citrus Variety Improvement Program was inaugurated and it was later renamed to Citrus Clonal Protection Program (CCPP). </p></font> 
   <font face="Helvetica, Arial"><br /> <p>Today The CCPP stands as a cooperative program with the <a href="http://www.ucr.edu/">University of California, Riverside</a> (UCR)-<a href="http://plantpathmicro.ucr.edu/">Department of Plant Pathology and Microbiology</a>, the <a href="http://www.cdfa.ca.gov/">California Department of Food and Agriculture</a> (CDFA), the <a href="http://www.aphis.usda.gov/">United States Department of Agriculture Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service</a> (USDA-APHIS) and the citrus industry of the state of California represented by the <a href="http://www.ccnb.info/">California Citrus Nursery Board</a> (CCNB) and the <a href="http://www.citrusresearch.org/">Citrus Research Board</a> (CRB). Since 2009, the CCPP has been a part of the <a href="http://nationalcleanplantnetwork.org/">National Clean Plant Network</a> (NCPN) for specialty crops. </p></font> 
   <font face="Helvetica, Arial"><br /> <p>The CCPP provides a safe mechanism for the introduction into California of citrus varieties from any citrus-growing area of the world for research, variety improvement, or for use by the commercial industry of the state. This mechanism includes, disease diagnosis and pathogen elimination followed by maintenance and distribution of true-to-type, primary citrus propagative material of the important fruit and rootstock varieties.</p></font>

     <h5> <em>Dr. Ye Li Invited to Give Keynote Presentation at Citizens University Committee Meeting</em></h5> 
     <p> <img alt="CSI MOU" src="http://soba.ucr.edu/images/news/174.jpg" style="width: 150px; height: 200px; margin: 5px; float: right;" /><em>(February 18, 2014)</em></p> 
     <p> Dr. Ye Li, Assistant Professor at the UCR School of Business Administration (SoBA), has been invited to share his research on how financial decisions are affected by age with the Citizens University Committee (CUC) on March 26th. Li, along with Columbia University colleagues, Martine Baldassi, Eric J. Johnson, and Elke U. Weber, conclude in their paper, “Complementary Cognitive Capabilities: Economic Decision Making, and Aging”,&nbsp; that older people are generally more adept at making financial decisions than their younger counterparts. Li’s research differentiates between fluid and crystallized intelligence, where fluid intelligence is the ability to learn and process information, and crystallized intelligence, which encapsulates accumulated knowledge and experience.</p> 
     <p> The CUC, whose objective is to assist UCR in development and community relations, meets monthly during the academic year and gives campus and community related updates during their meetings. These meetings are generally anchored by a UCR faculty or staff keynote presentation and are attended primarily by business and civic leaders. Li states, “I am very pleased to be invited to speak at the Citizens University Committee. I look forward to sharing my work and giving back to this wonderfully supportive community.”</p> 
     <p> Dr. Li’s keynote will take place on March 26<sup>th</sup> at the UCR Alumni and Visitor center at 7:30AM. A breakfast buffet will be served prior to the start of the meeting ($18 for CUC members and $22 for non-members). For more information about the Citizens University Committee or to RSVP to the upcoming event, please visit <a href="http://community.ucr.edu/citizens.html">community.ucr.edu/citizens.html</a>. &nbsp;</p>

     <p><strong><img class="img_L_borderless" src="/images/2011%20grad.jpg" alt="Ashley Vizenor" width="211" height="329" /></strong></p> 
     <p>Ashley Vizenor, is currently a 2nd year PhD Graduate student in Chemical and Environmental Engineering. She graduated from Cal State, Bernardino (CSUSB) in 2012 with a B.S in Chemistry and entered the CEE Graduate program in fall of 2012. &nbsp;She is currently the recipient of the 2012-2013 USDA/WRPI watershed Graduate Scholarship from the national institute of Food and Agriculture. Her current research at CE-CERT explores the impact of mixed gas-phase biogenic precursors for aerosol formation and particle water-uptake.</p> 
     <p><strong>Title:&nbsp;</strong><a href="http://aaarabstracts.com/2013/viewabstract.php?pid=643" target="_blank">Evaluation of Selective Ion Flow Tube Mass Spectrometry for Controlled Laboratory Studies. </a>ASHLEY VIZENOR, Chia-Li Chen, Derek Price, Mary Kacarab, Xinze Peng, Kelly McCoy, Igor Irianto, Shaokai Gao, David R. Cocker III, Akua Asa-Awuku, <em>University of California, Riverside<br /> <br />&nbsp;</em><strong>Abstract</strong><br /> Selective Ion Flow Tube Mass Spectrometry (SIFT-MS) is a form of direct mass spectrometry that utilizes precisely controlled chemical ionization reactions to detect and quantify trace amounts of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from whole air in real time, with typical detection limits at the part-per-trillion level (by volume – pptv). Chemical ionization is induced by one of three positively charged reagent ions: hydronium, nitric oxide, or diatomic oxygen. Soft-ionization reactions occur and the larger fragments of product ions are characterized and quantified. The instrument is operated in two modes: SIM (Selected Ion Mode) and mass modes. A mass scan takes a mass spectrum for each reagent ion, and can be used for identifying products from reactions. In a SIM scan, specific compounds are detected and quantified by detection of product ions. In the research presented, emphasis was placed on the SIM scan mode as accuracy and precision of quantification were the main goal of this study. A Syft Technologies Voice200 SIFT-MS was used to compare known amounts of VOCs with detected concentrations to better understand the accuracy and precision of the instrument. The compounds studied were 1-methylnapthalene, alpha-pinene, benzene, isoprene, m-xylene, naphthalene, toluene, beta-caryophyllene, and longifolene. The data show that SIFT-MS is a helpful tool for approximating concentrations of VOCs, as well as identifying products of reactions from the upper part-per-trillion to lower part-per-million range.</p>
