     <div align="center"> 
      <p><img src="images/welcome_image.gif" width="252" height="250" alt="Welcome Image" /></p> 
      <p>Established under the department of Comparative Literature and Foreign Languages, Classical Studies at UCR is a broadly based program, with specialization in the ancient languages and civilizations of the Greeks and Romans. Interdisciplinary courses supplement core offerings in Classical Studies, contributed by the departments of Art History, History, Philosophy, Political Science, Religious Studies, and Theatre. By studying Greco-Roman civilizations, Classical Studies students learn something of the foundations of Western languages and cultures and are in a position to recognize similarities with other world cultures, both ancient and modern. By studying ancient Greek and Latin, students also learn how to read, write, think, and communicate effectively and intelligently in English. How and why do civilizations rise, decline, and fall? How do cultures influence private ambitions as well as public duties? Which aspects of human nature endure through time? How does cultural background affect notions of nature, fate, the cosmos, and the divine? Ancient writers grappled with these same questions that so exercise our minds today.</p> 
      <p> <strong>What We Offer</strong></p> 
      <p>The major in Classical Studies leads to a Bachelor of Arts degree. The program also offers an approved subject matter preparation program for the Multiple Subject teaching credential. Students who complete such a program are exempt from subject matter examinations when applying for a teaching credential program. </p>

     <h5 style="line-height: 18px;"> <em>SoBA Global Programs Team Returns from India and Turkey</em></h5> 
     <p> <em>(January 21, 2014)<img alt="" src="http://soba.ucr.edu/images/news/169.jpg" style="height: 333px; margin: 5px; float: right;" /></em></p> 
     <p style="text-align: justify;"> For many college students, winter break conjures up images of rest, relaxation, and great food. Whether catching up with friends locally or back at home with family, academics are the least of students' worries. However, a group of UCR Business students spent this year's winter break doing the exact opposite.</p> 
     <p style="text-align: justify;"> From December 11-19, 2013, the&nbsp;SoBA Global Programs held their second trip to Mumbai, India concluding in Istanbul, Turkey.&nbsp; The week-long intensive exposed the team to the massive city of Mumbai, India's financial capital and Turkey's&nbsp;largest city located both in Europe and Asia.&nbsp; Corporate and cultural site visits included:</p> 
     <p> <strong>India</strong></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li> Tata Consultancy, one of the world's leading IT consulting firms</li> 
      <li> National Stock Exchange of India, India's largest exchange</li> 
      <li> The American Consulate</li> 
      <li> The Gateway of India, one of India's most famous colonial landmarks</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p> <strong>Turkey</strong></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li> The Blue Mosque and&nbsp;Hagia Sophia, among the world's largest and most famous mosques</li> 
      <li> Basilica Cistern, the world's largest underground cistern built in the 6th century</li> 
      <li> The Grand Bazaar, one of the oldest and largest covered markets in the world dating from the 15th century</li> 
      <li> Alarko&nbsp;Headquarters, Turkey's largest conglomerate company</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p> University of Mumbai, one of India's oldest and most distinguished universities, served as the host university.&nbsp; Students heard customized lectures from Indian faculty and practitioners on topics ranging from the history of India and Mumbai, India's liberalization, privatization, and globalization initiatives, and the future of the Indian economy.&nbsp; While in Istanbul, students enjoyed lectures on the history and future of Turkey by&nbsp;UCR MBA alum,&nbsp;Ece Baykut, now a brand management expert working in Istanbul.&nbsp; The program concluded with a&nbsp;personalized presentation by the CEO of&nbsp;Alarko Holdings.&nbsp;</p> 
     <p> The&nbsp;SoBA Global Programs is now enrolling for the next trip&nbsp;during spring break to&nbsp;University of Oxford where students will live in residence at Mansfield College&nbsp;taking part in UCR's customized program, <em>Oxford Young Entrepreneurial Leadership Program.&nbsp; </em>Enjoy the video of the &quot;UCR in India&quot;&nbsp;trip and visit the links below to learn more about&nbsp;SoBA's exciting trips to the world's most important markets</p> 
     <p> <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="//www.youtube.com/embed/Vlv-4NwqEYE?list=UUZCJwwpKDBQIvAaI_pBeymg" width="560"></iframe></p> 
     <ul> 
      <li> <a href="http://soba.ucr.edu/global/">UCR Global Programs</a> (Program information)</li> 
      <li> Please contact Dr. Sean Jasso to learn more (<a href="mailto:sean.jasso@ucr.edu">sean.jasso@ucr.edu</a>) about the global programs.</li>

      <h3>The People Behind Computing &amp; Communications</h3> 
      <p>The Associate Vice Chancellor of Computing &amp; Communications (C&amp;C) is responsible for the management and development of the computing and communications services on the campus of the University of California, Riverside. The Associate Vice Chancellor reports to the Executive Vice Chancellor &amp; Provost and manages several groups that provide services ranging from desktop computer support to enterprise application development to network security and firewall management. For more detailed information on C&amp;C department services, staff and contacts please select a department in the menu to the left.</p>

        <h2>Akua Asa-Awuku</h2> Assistant Professor of Chemical &amp; Environmental Engineering 
       </div> 
       <div class="pagePhoto"> 
        <img src="/images/profile/sustainability/profile-Akua-Asa-Awuku-banner.jpg" alt="Akua Asa-Awuku" width="956" height="355" /> 
       </div> 
       <div class="pageContent"> 
        <div class="profileLeft"> 
         <div class="researchSpotlight"> 
          <strong>Improving Air Quality</strong>
          <br /> Working in the world’s largest indoor air quality chamber, Akua Asa-Awuku looks at particle formation in the atmosphere and how it interacts with water, affecting cloud formation and global climate change. 
         </div> 
         <h3>Areas of Expertise</h3> 
         <div class="xpander"> 
          <strong>Areas of Expertise:</strong> 
          <ul> 
           <li>Chemical engineering</li> 
           <li>Atmospheric Particle Studies</li> 
          </ul> 
          <strong>College:</strong> 
          <ul> 
           <li><a href="http://www.engr.ucr.edu/">Bourns College of Engineering (BCOE)</a></li> 
          </ul> 
          <strong>Department:</strong> 
          <ul> 
           <li><a href="http://www.cee.ucr.edu/">Chemical/Environmental Engineering</a></li> 
          </ul> 
          <strong>Affiliations:</strong> 
          <ul>

         <h3>Research Summary</h3> 
         <div class="xpander">
           Understanding and predicting aerosol-cloud climate interactions. Specifically, the impact of warm cumulus clouds that may counteract the warming effects of greenhouse gases and models of cloud microphysical processes are investigated.
          <br />
          <br /> 
         </div> 
         <h3>Q&amp;A</h3> 
         <div class="null_xpander"> 
          <p><strong>Q: How do clouds play a role in climate?</strong><br /> Particle formation and composition affect cloud formation. Particles provide the seeds for water vapor to condense so clouds can form. Clouds reflect energy back into space, causing a cooling effect that can counteract what we see from greenhouse gasses that warm. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: How do particles affect air quality?</strong><br /> In terms of visibility on humid days, when we have a lot of particles in the atmosphere, the particles swell with water so we have less visibility. The role in which water condenses on those particles is part of the research I do, which is why I look at the composition of particles.<br /><br /> If we inhale particles, they have detrimental effects. We expose particles to high amounts of water vapor super saturation within our respiratory system, which can also form droplets and change the locations that they deposit in the respiratory system. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: What inspired you to pursue your research? </strong><br /> My previous background is in chemical engineering and I’ve always been interested in incorporating environmental aspects into what I do. I’m very concerned with the Earth and how we as humans can make a positive impact and understand the detrimental impacts. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: Why should everyone be concerned with climate change? </strong><br />I understand not everyone may be concerned with climate change but everyone should be concerned with air quality, and the two are very much intertwined. The components that affect air quality also impact the atmosphere and climate. Climate change will significantly impact the way we live – our economics, transportation, our quality of life. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: What is the best part of doing your research at UCR?</strong> <br /> Having the tools we need to succeed. UC Riverside’s College of Engineering Center for Environmental Research &amp; Technology (CE-CERT) has the world’s largest indoor air quality chamber. It is temperature- and pressure-controlled allowing us to simulate particle formation in the atmosphere. In addition, we have advanced instrumentation to look at the chemistry and physics of particles we form including the chemicals of the gas phase and physical properties such as water uptake and volatility of the particles. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: How does the chamber work?</strong> <br /> We’re able to inject gas phase species and oxidants like ozone and see how they react with the precursor gas we inject to form low volatility condense phase materials that form particles, and we watch how they evolve in our chamber. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: What is the future of air quality research?</strong><br /> We've made many advances in air quality however much is still not understood about the composition and fate of gas-phase and particles in the atmosphere. The emissions and evolution of trace species that can be detrimental to health and their links to human respiratory symptoms and diseases must be quantified. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: How is California doing? Are we making progress?</strong><br /> We have over 50 years of atmospheric expertise at UC Riverside. Many important results and findings from the research done at UCR have been incorporated into state and federal regulations. The air quality in the Inland Empire has vastly improved but there is still more we can do. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: Why is UC Riverside a great place to work?</strong><br /> We have a community that truly cares. From undergraduate to graduate students, faculty and administration, we all care about making a difference where we live and where we work. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: What does Living the Promise mean to you?</strong> <br /> To me, Living the Promise is being true to science, following problems that matter and finding solutions that make a difference. <br /><br /> <strong>Q: What advice do you have for students graduating in the next five years?</strong><br /> I would advise students to hone their talents and focus their efforts on to something they care about. Passion is important and having the expertise to apply it really counts, no matter what the profession.</p>
