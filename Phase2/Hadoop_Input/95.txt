        <td rowspan="2"> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/hoddle/index.html">Mark S. Hoddle, Ph.D.</a></h3> <p><em>Biological Control Specialist and Principal Investigator&nbsp;</em></p> <p>Dr. Hoddle has headed the research in this laboratory since 1997 and is primarily involved in the identification of pest problems wf biological control could be a successful approach. The location, release and evaluation of natural enemy impacts on population growth features strongly in his research. The evaluation of biological control agents are conducted primarily in the field and, when necessary, aspects of both pest and natural enemy biology and behavior are studied in the laboratory.</p> </td>

        <td rowspan="2"> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/irvin/index.html">Nicola A. Irvin, Ph.D.</a></h3> <p><em>Biological Control Specialist and Research Scholar</em></p> <p>Dr. Irvin joined the Hoddle laboratory in 2001 as a postdoctoral scholar. She has a heavy focus on researching the glassy-winged sharpshooter, <em>Homalodisca vitripennis</em> (Germar), and its mymarid wasp biological control agents <em>Gonatocerus ashmeadi</em>, <em>G. fasciatus</em>, <em>G. triguttatus</em> and <em>G. tuberculifemur</em>.&nbsp;Dr. Irvin has since been promoted to Assistant Specialist and in 2007 was awarded a Western Region Sustainable Agriculture Research and Education (Western SARE) grant to investigate the use of nectar cover crops for sustainable pest control in California vineyards.&nbsp;</p> </td>

        <td><a class="img_link" href="/christina_hoddle.html"><img class="img_L" title="Christina Hoddle" src="/images/christina_hoddle_ucr.jpg" alt="Christina Hoddle" width="150" height="182" /></a></td> 
        <td> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/christina_hoddle.html">Christina Hoddle</a></h3> <p><em>Assistant Specialist</em></p> <p>Christina has collaborated with the Hoddle laboratory since 2004. Christina has dedicated her research efforts towards biological control and invasive species with an emphasis on crop pests. She has been involved in a variety of international projects sponsored by various granting agencies, private institutions, and commodity boards. An example of these finished and on-going projects include: hunting for bean thrips in Australia (Funded: Citrus Research Board-CRB), identification and production of pheromone for avocado seed moth (<em>Stenoma catenifer</em>) in Guatemala and Peru (Funded: CA Avocado Commission), searching for Asian citrus psyllid parasitoids in Pakistan (Funded: CDFA/CRB), monitoring&nbsp;<em>Rodolia cardinalis</em>&nbsp;populations on the Galapagos Islands and assessing the impact of this biocontrol agent on the cottony cushion scale,&nbsp;<em>Icerya purchasi</em>&nbsp;(Funded: Various Donors), red palm weevil (<em>Rhynchophorus ferrugineus</em>&nbsp;and<em>R. vulneratus</em>) flight activity in Saudi Arabia (Funded: King Faisal University) and genetic analyses throughout southeast Asia (Funded: CDFA).&nbsp;</p> </td> 
       </tr>

        <td><a class="img_link" href="/rugman-jones.html"><img class="img_L" title="Paul Rugman-Jones" src="/images/paul_rugman_jones_ucr.jpg" alt="Paul Rugman-Jones" width="150" height="182" /></a></td> 
        <td> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/rugman-jones.html">Paul F. Rugman-Jones, PhD</a></h3> <p><em>Associate Project Scientist</em></p> <p>Dr. Rugman-Jones's research at UCR focuses on the problem posed by invasive insect pests to U.S. agriculture. Specifically, he is involved in the molecular identification and characterization of exotic insect pests and their natural enemies.</p> <p>Based in the Stouthamer lab at UCR, Dr. Rugman-Jones exploits variations in the DNA sequences of individuals to: resolve taxonomic and diagnostic issues; investigate population structure; and, identify the area of origin, and routes of entry, of exotic pests. The accurate identification of pests and natural enemies is fundamental to encouraging fair trade, selecting appropriate mitigation measures, and developing successful biological control programs.</p> </td>

        <td rowspan="2"> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/ricky_lara.html">Ricky Lara</a></h3> <p><em>Ph.D. Candidate</em></p> <p>Ricky joined the Hoddle laboratory in 2008 as a graduate student pursuing a Ph.D. in Entomology. His academic interests revolve around acarology, agroecology and integrated management of economically important pests in agriculture. This committed appreciation of acarology began with several undergraduate internships working in California vineyards.</p> </td>

        <td> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/vanessa_lopez.html">Vanessa Lopez</a></h3> <p><em>Ph.D. Candidate</em></p> <p>Vanessa is a Ph.D. graduate student in the Entomology Department who joined the Hoddle Lab in September 2009. She is currently working on developing a classical biological control program for the invasive goldspotted oak borer (<em>Agrilus auroguttatus</em>). She has also previously done work researching the hitchhiking behavior and load selection of leaf-cutter ants (<em>Atta cephalotes</em>) in Monteverde, Costa Rica as an undergraduate in UCR's Education Abroad Program.</p> </td>

        <td><a class="img_link" href="/bistline-east.html"><img class="img_L" title="Allison Bistline-East" src="/images/allison_bistline_ucr.jpg" alt="Allison Bistline-East" width="150" height="182" /></a></td> 
        <td> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/bistline-east.html">Allison Bistline-East</a></h3> <p><em>Graduate Student Researcher</em></p> <p>Allison entered the PhD program in Entomology in 2013, but her ties to the Hoddle lab stretch back much farther. While an undergraduate, she worked in Dr. Paul de Ley's lab (UCR Department of Nematology) researching marine and freshwater nematode ecology. After receiving her bachelor's degree in 2008, she left the De Ley lab and was hired by the Hoddle lab to assist on glassy-winged sharpshooter (GWSS) research being conducted in organic vineyards. After the GWSS project came to an end, Allison was transferred to a new project focusing on the Asian citrus psyllid (ACP),<em>Diaphorena citri</em>. She has been working with ACP and its associated natural enemies from Pakistan (<em>Tamarixia radiata</em>&nbsp;(Hymenoptera: Eulophidae) and&nbsp;<em>Diaphorencyrtus aligarhensis</em>&nbsp;(Hymneoptera: Encyrtidae)) for the past 3 years, and is continuing this research as she pursues her PhD.</p> </td> 
       </tr>

        <td rowspan="3"> <h3><a class="img_link" href="/schall.html">Kelsey Schall</a></h3> <p><em>Graduate Student Researcher</em></p> <p><span>Kelsey completed a project that examined the effect of Argentine ant density in citrus trees on successful parasitism of&nbsp;</span><em>D. citri</em><span>&nbsp;by&nbsp;</span><em>T. radiata</em><span>. She also looked at the impact of a drop in Argentine ant density on host switching from tending ACP to tending more “attractive” citrus honeydew producers such as brown scale and mealybug. Her current research focuses on host competition between two&nbsp;</span><em>D. citri</em><span>&nbsp;parasitoids, the aforementioned&nbsp;</span><em>T. radiata</em><span>&nbsp;and&nbsp;</span><em>Diaporencyrtus aligarhensis</em><span>. She hopes to determine if this interaction is a contributing factor to the lack of establishment of&nbsp;</span><em>D. aligarhensis</em><span>&nbsp;in Florida.</span></p> </td>

      <h3>Course Requisites Overview</h3> 
      <p>In order to successfully register for a course, you may need to meet certain requirements that must be completed prior to enrolling in a course. These range from the prior completion of foundational courses to obtaining instructor approval. The most common course requisites are prerequisites, co-requisites and restrictions. </p> 
      <div class="wizard-wrapper"> 
       <div class="wizard-content border1px" style="margin-top: 20px; padding-top: 20px; width: 379px; min-height: 310px; float: right; margin-right: 20px"> 
        <div id="tab1" class="verticalslider_contents verticalSliderContentPadleft20"> 
         <h3>Prerequisites</h3> 
         <p> <br /> A prerequisite is a required preparatory course or placement exam score that must be completed prior to enrolling in a course. <br /> <br /> Course prerequisites are outlined in the <a href="/registrar/schedule-of-classes/classes.aspx">Schedule of Classes </a>and <a href="/registrar/schedule-of-classes/catalog.aspx">Catalog</a>. <br /> <br /> If you completed work outside of UCR, transfer work is articulated to UCR course work as appropriate and will therefore satisfy prerequisite requirements. Please talk to your academic advisor and the department offering your course if you have any questions.<br /> <br /> Learn more about <a href="http://placementtest.ucr.edu">placement exams.</a> </p> 
        </div> 
        <div id="tab2" class="verticalslider_contents verticalSliderContentPadleft20"> 
         <h3>Co-requisites </h3> 
         <p> <br /> If a course has co-requisites, it means that the course is comprised of two or more sections that you must register for simultaneously. For example: lecture and discussion; lecture and laboratory; or lecture, discussion, and workshop; BIOL 005A and BIOL 05LA; CHEM 001A and CHEM 01LA. <br /> <br /> Co-requisite sections are lecture-specific. For example, if you register for MATH 009B, lecture section 001, you may only be able to register for discussion sections 002, 003 or 004. <br /> <br /> If you do not register for all of a course’s co-requisite sections, you will receive an error message (e.g., <strong>“DIS-REGISTRATION REQUIRED FOR LEC”</strong>). <br /> <br /> “<strong>LCA</strong>” means <strong>“additional lecture”</strong> and is typically associated with specific math courses. </p> 
        </div> 
        <div id="tab3" class="verticalslider_contents verticalSliderContentPadleft20"> 
         <h3>Restrictions </h3> 
         <p> <br /> A restriction on a class means that only certain students may register for it; for example, only juniors or only art majors. <br /> <br /> You may need to get an instructor’s approval before you can register for a restricted class. </p> 
        </div>
