          <td> <h3>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; <strong>Department of Computer Science &amp; Engineering<br /></strong></h3> <p>&nbsp;</p> <p>Welcome to Computer Science and Engineering (CSE) at the <a href="http://www.ucr.edu/">University of California, Riverside</a>!</p> <p>The department offers the B.S. degree in <a href="/education/undergraduate/csmajor/">Computer Science</a>, and <a href="/education/undergraduate/bunfmajor/">Business Informatics</a>, the <a href="/education/graduate/program/">M.S. and Ph.D. degrees</a> in Computer Science, and we participate in the <a href="http://cen.ucr.edu">Computer Engineering</a> Program. We are also proud to introduce our new <a href="http://cmsstudent.engr.ucr.edu/policies/requirements/combinedBSMS.html">Five-Year Joint B.S.+M.S. degree</a> in Computer Science, the Five-Year Joint B.S.+M.S. degree in Computer Engineering and the <a href="http://cen.ucr.edu">M.S. degree in Computer Engineering</a>. We have strong research programs in architecture, compilers, embedded systems, algorithms, computational biology, databases, data mining, computer networks, distributed processing, artificial intelligence, software engineering, and graphics.</p> <p>CSE students at UCR enjoy the benefits of a smaller campus, which include a more relaxed and personal atmosphere with specialized attention and guidance. In addition, our students can also take advantage of the innovative research, first-rate professors, and worldwide networking that comes from being part of the prestigious UC System. Our department currently has five lecturers and <a href="http://www1.cs.ucr.edu/people/faculty/"><span style="color: blue;">23 tenure-track faculty</span></a> that include three ACM Fellows, four IEEE Fellows, five AAAS Fellows, one Fellow of AAAI, one NSF Presidential Young Investigator, one AFOSR Young Investigator, and ten NSF CAREER award holders. Although relatively young, we have quickly become a well-established and well known research department, with <a href="http://www1.cs.ucr.edu/people/current_grads/"><span style="color: blue;">194 graduate students (61 M.S. and 133 Ph.D.)</span></a> enrolled in the 2013-14 academic year. We graduate more than 20 Ph.D. students per year and place them in highly respected organizations. Our new external grants for 2013-14 academic year exceeds $10,000,000.</p> <p>The Department is pleased to announce a significant increase in the ranking of our program from such organizations as the National Research Council (NRC), Princeton Review, and U.S. News &amp; World Report. Ph.Ds.org showed an independent analysis of the NRC data and rated us very high as well. We anticipate an increase in student applications from prospective graduates utilizing this information and are looking forward to a successful recruiting season.</p> <p>I invite you to further explore our department and the exciting opportunities that we provide on our web site and again, welcome you to Computer Science and Engineering at UCR!</p> <p><strong>Laxmi N. Bhuyan<br />Distinguished Professor and Chair, CSE Department</strong></p>

      <h3><a name="access"> </a> Wireless network availability at UCR:</h3> 
      <p>UCR provides two ways of connecting to the wireless network. The way you connect to the wireless network will depend on your desire for security and the hardware capabilities of your wireless NIC (Network Interface Card). Below is a brief description of each network, which is referenced by the SSID (Service Set Identifier).</p> 
      <ul> 
       <li>The &quot;<strong>ucrwpa</strong>&quot; SSID is an WPA2 encrypted network. Users on this network will authenticate using&nbsp;a wireless&nbsp;client utility&nbsp;that is provided by your wireless hardware vendor or an open-source vendor. The user's data is encrypted from the wireless device to the UCR campus network and cannot be &quot;sniffed&quot; by hackers. It is recommended for use while on campus as it is more secure than Mobilenet. 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Note: ucrwpa is a more secure and complex network and requires some effort to configure.</strong></li> 
        </ul> </li> 
       <li>The <strong>&quot;mobilenet&quot;</strong> SSID is an open, non-encrypted network. This network has a browser based captive portal that authenticates users. Users must use VPN or application based security methods (SSL, SSH) to protect personal information.<strong><br /></strong> 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Note: mobilenet is a coffee house type public wireless network and is relatively easy to configure and connect to.</strong></li> 
        </ul> </li> 
       <li><strong>eduroam</strong> (education roaming) is the secure, world-wide roaming wireless access service developed for the international research and education community. eduroam, which is an encrypted (WPA2) wireless service,&nbsp;allows students, researchers, faculty, and staff from UCR to obtain internet connectivity across campus and when visiting other participating institutions.</li> 
       <li>To start using the UCR wireless network, you need&nbsp;all of the following: 
        <ul> 
         <li>A wireless capable Desktop, Notebook, iPhone or PDA</li> 
         <li>Wireless network interface card (NIC) that adheres to the IEEE 802.11a, 802.11g, and/or 802.11n standards</li> 
         <li>Choose which network (<a title="Mobilenet" href="/wireless/mobilenet.html">mobilenet</a>, <a title="Mobilenet" href="/wireless/ucrwpa.html">ucrwpa, or eduroam</a>) to use. Read the configuration pages</li> 
         <li>UCR Net ID - <em><strong>Faculty and staff</strong></em> will use their UCR Net ID to login to the wireless network. Please read the <a href="/esai/index.php?content=ucrnetid.html">UCR Net ID</a> information on the ESAI website for more information. <em><strong>Students </strong></em>already have wireless accounts set up automatically when they first enter UCR. Students may log in with their UCR Net ID and Permanent PIN. <em><strong>Guests</strong></em> may obtain wireless access by having their host contact the Computer Support Group.</li> 
        </ul> </li> 
      </ul>

     <p><a name="Top of Page"> </a></p> 
     <div id="omnicontent"> 
      <h3><a href="http://cisr.ucr.edu/asian_citrus_psyllid.html"><img id="placeholder3" class="img_L" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/asian_citrus_psyllid_thumb.jpg" alt="Asian Citrus Psyllid" title="Asian Citrus Psyllid" /> Asian Citrus Psyllid</a></h3> 
      <p>Asian citrus psyllid (ACP) is an efficient vector of the citrus disease huanglongbing (HLB), previously called citrus greening disease, which is one of the most destructive diseases of citrus worldwide. The psyllid is under eradication in Southern California. If the psyllid and the disease were to become established in California, the disease would devastate the citrus industry.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/hoddle/asphondylia_websteri.html"><img id="placeholder8" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/avocado_fruit_galling_midge_thumb.jpg" alt="Avocado Fruit Galling Midge" title="Avocado Fruit Galling Midge" width="132" height="85" /> Avocado Fruit Galling Midge</a></h3> 
      <p><em>Asphondylia websteri, </em>the alfalfa gall midge, is a polyphagous galling midge that attacks immature fruiting bodies of native and exotic crop plants in the southwestern United States. This midge emerged as a pest of cultivated legumes in the southwest in the early 1900's where larvae caused damaged to pods by feeding in the ovary and immature fruiting bodies.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/hoddle/pdfs/avocado_lace_bug.pdf"><img id="placeholder9" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/avocado_lace_bug_thumb.jpg" alt="Avocado Lace Bug" title="Avocado Lace Bug" width="132" height="85" />Avocado Lace Bug</a></h3> 
      <p>Avocado lace bug, <em>Pseudacysta perseae</em>, was first described in Florida in 1908. The common name &quot;lace bug&quot; is derived from the reticulate lace-like patterning of the thorax and wings of adults. There are around 154 species of lace bug found in North America. Some lace bug species in the US are important ornamental pests attacking azaleas and rhododendrons.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/hoddle/trioza/trioza.html"><img id="placeholder9" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/avocado_leaf_galling_psyllid_thumb.jpg" alt="Avocado Leaf Galling Psyllid" title="Avocado Leaf Galling Psyllid" width="132" height="85" />Avocado Leaf Galling Psyllid</a></h3> 
      <p>Four species of leaf galling psyllid (<em>Trioza</em>) are currently known to attack avocado foliage and nymphal feeding causes some form of leaf deformation. All four species appear to have an extremely restricted host range and have only been recorded from leaves of avocados. Under some circumstances certain species may even be restricted to particular races of avocado.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/stenoma/stenoma.html"><img id="placeholder12" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/stenoma_thumb_132.jpg" alt="Stenoma catenifer" title="Stenoma catenifer" width="132" height="85" /></a><a href="/stenoma/stenoma.html"> Avocado Seed Moth</a></h3> 
      <p>Avocado thrips were first discovered in California in 1996 as a result of damaged foliage in a Saticoy avocado orchard in Ventura County. Since this initial discovery, the thrips population has increased rapidly causing significant damage to foliage and fruit. Avocado thrips have not been recorded in any other host plant in California, suggesting a limited host plant range.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/hoddle/avocadothrips.html"><img id="placeholder12" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/invasive_species_thumbnails/avacado_thrip.jpg" alt="Avocado Thrips" title="Avocado Thrips" width="132" height="85" /> Avocado Thrips</a></h3> 
      <p>Avocado thrips were first discovered in California in 1996 as a result of damaged foliage in a Saticoy avocado orchard in Ventura County. Since this initial discovery, the thrips population has increased rapidly causing significant damage to foliage and fruit. Avocado thrips have not been recorded in any other host plant in California, suggesting a limited host plant range.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/irvin/research/wsare.html"><img id="placeholder" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/cover_crops_as_sustaninable_pest_control_thumb.jpg" alt="Cover Crops as Sustainable Pest Control" title="Cover Crops as Sustainable Pest Control" width="132" height="85" /> Cover Crops as Sustainable Pest Control</a></h3> 
      <p>Work recorded on this webpage is the first significant set of studies that have investigated the strengths and limitations of cover cropping under the unique growing conditions representative of grape producing areas of Southern California. The project investigated the use of buckwheat and cahaba vetch for management of arthropod grape pests in Temecula, California.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/cryptaspasma/cryptaspasma.html"><img id="placeholder" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/cryptaspasma_thumb.jpg" alt="Cryptaspasma sp." title="Cryptaspasma sp." width="132" height="85" /></a> <a href="/cryptaspasma/cryptaspasma.html">Cryptaspasma sp.</a></h3> 
      <p>Very little is known about the ecology and biology of <em>Cryptaspasma </em>sp. Determining the pest status of <em>Cryptaspasma </em>sp. attacking avocados in countries exporting fruit is important for understanding the risk this insect poses to countries importing avocado fruit from areas that have endemic populations of this avocado seed-feeding moth.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/goldspotted_oak_borer.html"><img id="placeholder" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/goldspotted_oak_borer_thumb.jpg" alt="Goldspotted Oak Borer" title="Goldspotted Oak Borer" width="132" height="85" /> Goldspotted Oak Borer</a></h3> 
      <p>The goldspotted oak borer (GSOB) was first detected in 2004 in San Diego County, California by the California Department of Food and Agriculture during a survey for exotic woodborers. Since then, GSOB has contributed to the mortality of more than 21,500 trees over approximately 4,900 km<sup>2</sup>, and this infested area continues to increase as the GSOB population grows and spreads. </p> 
      <h3><a href="/irvin/gwssbiocontrol.html"><img id="placeholder12" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/invasive_species_thumbnails/glassy_winged_sharpshooter.jpg" alt="GWSS" title="GWSS" width="132" height="85" /> Glassy-Winged Sharpshooter</a></h3> 
      <p>The glassy-winged sharpshooter (GWSS), likely introduced from the southeastern U.S. as eggs on nursery stock, was first observed in Orange and Ventura counties in California in 1989. It has a large plant-host range and is especially abundant on citrus.</p> 
      <p>&nbsp;</p> 
      <h3><a href="/hoddle/persea_mite.html"><img id="placeholder9" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/persea_thumb.jpg" alt="Persea Mite" title="Persea Mite" width="132" height="85" />Persea Mite</a></h3> 
      <p>Persea mite, <em>Oligonychus perseae</em>, was described in 1975 from specimens collected from avocado foliage intercepted from Mexico in El Paso, TX. In California, persea mite was first discovered attacking avocados in San Diego County in 1990. Contaminated fruit bins, harvesting equipment and clothing assisted in the rapid dispersal of persea mite throughout California.</p> 
      <h3><a href="/hoddle/red_banded_whitefly.html"><img id="placeholder9" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/red_banded_whitefly_thumb.jpg" alt="Red Banded Whitefly" title="Red Banded Whitefly" width="132" height="85" />Red Banded Whitefly</a></h3> 
      <p>Although a minor pest, honeydew production can promote the growth of black sooty mold on leaves and feeding by adult whiteflies can deform immature leaves. Attaining effective biological control of <em>Tetraleurodes perseae</em> is critical if this pest is to be constrained at densities which do not cause economic damage.</p>

      <h3><a href="/irvin/gwssbiocontrol.html"><img id="placeholder12" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="http://cisr.ucr.edu/images/invasive_species_thumbnails/glassy_winged_sharpshooter.jpg" alt="GWSS" title="GWSS" width="132" height="85" /></a> <a href="/irvin/gwssbiocontrol.html">Glassy-Winged Sharpshooter</a></h3> 
      <p>The glassy-winged sharpshooter (GWSS), likely introduced from the southeastern U.S. as eggs on nursery stock, was first observed in Orange and Ventura counties in California in 1989. It has a large plant-host range and is especially abundant on citrus.</p> 
      <p>&nbsp;</p> 
      <h3><a href="/boyd/pubs_posters/ccbcv_proceedings_2006.pdf"><img id="placeholder" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/non-target_impact_studies_thumb.jpg" alt="Non-Target Impact Studies" title="Non-Target Impact Studies" width="132" height="85" /> Non-Target Impact Studies</a></h3> 
      <p>&nbsp;</p> 
      <p>&nbsp;</p> 
      <p>&nbsp;</p> 
      <h3><a href="/wampler/wampler-bgss-poster_esa-march_2009_final.pdf"><img id="placeholder" class="img_L" style="float: left;" src="/images/vibrational_mating_calls_thumb.jpg" alt="Blue-Green Sharpshooter" title="Blue-Green Sharpshooter" width="132" height="85" /> Vibrational Mating Calls of the Blue-Green Sharpshooter</a></h3> 
      <p>The blue-green sharpshooter (BGSS) is a xylem-feeding leafhopper that vectors the bacterium <em>Xylella fastidiosa</em>, the causative agent of Pierce’s disease in grapes. This sharpshooter is native to the western United States where it is found commonly throughout much of California. Although this pest has been problematic for over 100 years, little basic research has been conducted on it.</p>
