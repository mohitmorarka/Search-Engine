     <h3>Policy</h3> 
     <p> The University of California, Riverside is committed to ensuring the privacy and accuracy of your confidential information to the extent possible, subject to provisions of state and federal law. Other than as required by laws that guarantee public access to certain types of information, or in response to subpoenas or other legal instruments that authorize access, personally-identifiable information is not actively shared. In particular, we do not re-distribute or sell personal information collected on our web servers. </p> 
     <h3>Information Collected</h3> 
     <p> UCR websites may collect personal information such as name, address, e-mail address, telephone number(s), and/or educational interests. Such personal information may be requested by us for research, public service or teaching programs, or for administrative purposes. Additional personal information, such as credit card account information, may be requested for purchases or enrollment purposes. </p> 
     <p> Web servers typically collect, at least temporarily, the following information: Internet Protocol (IP) address of computer being used; web pages requested; referring web page; browser used; date and time. UCR may collect statistics identifying particular IP addresses from which our websites are accessed. </p> 
     <h3>Use of Collected Information</h3> 
     <p> UCR may use personal information collected from websites for the purpose of future communication back to online enrollees, in order to keep you informed of such activities as campus programs, symposia and/or special events, but only if you are provided the opportunity to opt out of that type of use. </p> 
     <p> UCR may use browser-IP-address information and anonymous-browser history to report information about site accesses and for statistical purposes. This information is generally used to improve Web presentation and utilization. The campus also may use IP address information for troubleshooting purposes. </p> 
     <p> Some UCR online activity sites may use &quot;cookies&quot; in order to deliver web content specific to individual users' interests or to keep track of online purchasing transactions. Sensitive personal information is not stored within cookies. </p> 
     <h3>Distribution of Collected Information</h3> 
     <p> UCR will not disclose, without your consent, personal information collected about you, except for certain explicit circumstances in which disclosure is required by law. </p> 
     <p> UCR will not distribute or sell personal information to third-party organizations. </p> 
     <h3>Individual Choice</h3> 
     <p> Individuals who wish to use methods other than online enrollment may submit requests by e-mail or U.S. mail addressed to the UC Riverside organization responsible for the website. </p> 
     <h3>Access to Your Own Information</h3> 
     <p> Questions regarding users' rights to review, modify or delete their previously provided personal information should be directed to the campus organization to which they provided the information. Any disputes will be resolved under existing records regulations applicable to UCR. </p> 
     <h3>Main Campus Site</h3> 
     <p> The main campus site (i.e. www.ucr.edu) in addition to general logs, also collects usage information the links on its pages. This information is only used in an aggregate form to determine how resources are used. </p>

     <h2 class="intro"> You’re ‘In’</h2> 
     <h3 class="verd intro"> You could get guaranteed admission to our <br /> top-ranking university. Here’s how.</h3> 
     <p> Not only is UC Riverside one of the most ethnically diverse research universities in the nation, it's a selective UC campus. That's why guaranteed admission is such an incredible opportunity.</p> 
     <p> By following the path laid out by our two guarantee programs, high school and transfer students can reserve a place at one of California's premier universities.</p> 
     <p> Whether you're a community college student hoping to transfer or a high school junior planning ahead — you have a unique chance to secure your future.</p>

        <h3 class="twoStepOrange"> <span>High School Juniors:</span> <br /> Guarantee Your Admission </h3> 
        <p> Reserve your spot at UC Riverside before anyone else can even apply! </p> 
        <p> UC Riverside is the only UC that offers the Guaranteed Admission Program (GAP) for outstanding high school juniors. Unlike early decision programs, GAP is nonbinding. You may apply, be admitted and then choose whether or not to attend. </p> 
        <h4> Qualify for GAP </h4> 
        <ul> 
         <li>Complete 13 of 15 <a href="http://www.ucop.edu/agguide/">UC-approved &quot;a-g&quot; courses</a> by the end of your junior year. (Finish fourth-year English and either performing arts or an elective as a senior). </li> 
         <li>Earn a minimum (weighted) 3.9 GPA in all &quot;a-g&quot; coursework (and maintain academic excellence as a senior). </li> 
         <li>Score at least 1600 on the SAT Reasoning exam or 23 on the ACT Plus Writing. SAT Subject exams are not required, but can be a bonus. </li> 
        </ul> 
        <h4> Apply for GAP</h4> 
        <ul> 
         <li>Complete your free online GAP application at <a href="http://gap.ucr.edu">gap.ucr.edu</a> <em>during the enrollment period</em> of <strong>June 16-July 31, 2014</strong>. Find answers to FAQs there, too. </li> 
         <li>You still must submit a <a href="http://www.universityofcalifornia.edu/admissions/how-to-apply/apply-online/index.html"> UC application</a> by November of your senior year even if you are admitted to UCR through GAP. </li> 
        </ul> 
        <h4> Questions?</h4> 
        <p> Do I have to send my transcripts? Do I have to take the SAT Subject Tests? Find fast answers to these and other frequently asked questions, <a class="inline" href="#inline_content"> here </a>. </p> 
        <h4> Contact Us</h4> 
        <p> Email a UCR admissions counselor at <a href="mailto:admissions@ucr.edu">admissions@ucr.edu</a> or call (951) 827-3411. </p>

        <h3 class="twoStepOrange"> <span>Transfer Students:</span> <br /> Guarantee Your Admission </h3> 
        <p> Ensure your admission to one of the top-ranked universities in the country. Our Transfer Admission Guarantee (TAG) program allows eligible students to study at a California community college and have a guarantee of admission to UCR. </p> 
        <p> Use the <a href="http://www.universityofcalifornia.edu/admissions/transfer/guarantee/index.html"> UC Transfer Admissions Planner (TAP)</a> to enter your completed and planned coursework from the beginning of your college career to help you track your progress toward meeting UCR’s minimum requirements. The TAP also serves as your TAG application and allows staff to communicate important information to you. </p> 
        <p> Submit your TAG application <strong>September 1-30, 2013 </strong>for Fall Quarter 2014. <u>You may only TAG to one UC</u>. </p> 
        <h4> 1. Meet the Following Criteria at the Time of Application:</h4> 
        <ul> 
         <li>Complete a minimum of 30 UC-transferable semester units.</li> 
         <li>Earn the minimum required GPA for your intended major: 
          <ul> 
           <li>3.0 for College of Humanities and Social Sciences</li> 
           <li>3.1 plus major preparation for Bourns College of Engineering</li> 
           <li>3.1 plus major preparation for College of Natural and Agricultural Sciences</li> 
           <li>3.2 plus major preparation is required for Biology, Biochemistry, Chemistry and Neuroscience.</li> 
           <li>3.0 plus major preparation for School of Business Administration</li> 
          </ul> </li> 
         <li>Complete the First English Composition (UC-E) by Fall 2014 and Second English Composition by Spring 2015.</li> 
         <li>Non-selecting majors must complete a minimum of one UC-transferable math (UC-M) by Fall 2013.</li> 
         <li>You must be a U.S. citizen, permanent resident or AB540-qualified.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p> Only students transferring in directly from a California Community College (CCC) are considered for a TAG, including international students (with a visa). The University of California defines a CCC transfer student as one who has completed more than half of their units at a CCC.</p> 
        <h4> 2. Complete Your TAG Contract </h4> 
        <ul> 
         <li>You must complete 60 semester (90 quarter) transferable units by the end of spring 2014. Summer course work will not be accepted to meet minimum admission requirements or major preparation. </li> 
         <li>You must complete required major preparation for your intended major. See major selection criteria at&nbsp;<a href="http://www.assist.org/" target="_blank">www.assist.org</a>.</li> 
         <li>You must maintain the required GPA for your intended major (see above). </li> 
        </ul> 
        <h4> 3. Submit a UC Application </h4> 
        <p> Submit your <a href="http://www.universityofcalifornia.edu/admissions/how-to-apply/apply-online/index.html"> UC application</a> during the <strong>November 1-30 filing period</strong> and apply for your contracted major and term.</p> 
        <h4> 4. Enjoy Guaranteed Admission to UCR!</h4> 
        <p> If you completed the provisions of your TAG contract and submitted a UC application: Welcome to UCR! <strong></strong> </p> 
        <h4> Questions?</h4> 
        <p> <a href="mailto:transfer@ucr.edu">Email a UCR admissions counselor</a> or call (951) 827-3411.</p>

       <h4>GAP Frequently Asked Questions</h4> 
       <p> <br /> <b>Q: If I am admitted to GAP, do I have to attend UCR?</b><br /> A: No, GAP is non-binding. You may apply, be admitted and attend any institution of your choice.<br /> <br /> <b>Q: Do I have to send in transcripts with your application?</b><br /> A: No, all of the information on the GAP application is self-reported; we do not require a copy of your transcripts. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: Is there cost for GAP?</b><br /> A: No, there is no application fee for GAP. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: Is the GPA weighted?</b><br /> A: Yes, the GPA for GAP is weighted. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: Do I have to take the SAT Subject Tests?</b><br /> A: We do not require the SAT Subject Tests for GAP. Completion of the Subject Tests can be considered a bonus in the evaluation process.<br /> <br /> <b>Q: Do I have to fulfill all of the requirements? Can I have a little higher GPA and lower SAT scores or vise versa? </b><br /> A: You must meet both the GPA and test score requirements to be admitted to GAP without exception. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: Can I take a class over the summer to fulfill the requirements?</b><br /> A: Yes, you can fulfill the GAP requirements with summer school courses. You will have to send us your grades from the summer school courses as soon as you receive them. <br /><br /> <b>Q: Do I have to submit the UC application in November?</b><br /> A: Yes, you must submit a UC application and apply to UCR. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: Do I have to write an essay to be admitted to GAP?</b><br /> A: No, we do not require an essay for GAP, but you will have to write a personal statement for the UC application in November. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: How many positions do you have open to guarantee students?</b><br /> A: We are not limited as to how many students we can guarantee admission to as long as the student meets the program requirements. <br /> <br /> <b>Q: Does any other UC offer this type of guaranteed program?</b><br /> A: No, UCR is the only campus that offers guaranteed admission. <br /> </p>

      <div class="span4 hero-unit"> 
       <p>We provide strategic leadership, planning and support for undergraduate admissions, student financial support and student services.</p> 
      </div> 
      <div class="span4">
       <p>Student Affairs supports UC campuses, K-12 schools, California community colleges and community organizations in their efforts to prepare, matriculate, support and enhance the academic, social and intellectual life of a diverse current and future student body. We provide policy analysis and development, research, legislative and regulatory analysis, and program management. We also advise and support campuses in maintaining positive campus climates that are conducive to student success, diversity, health and wellness, leadership and involvement, and student retention.</p>

      <p>RIVERSIDE, Calif. (<a href="http://www.ucr.edu/">www.ucr.edu</a>) — A team of researchers from the University of California, Riverside’s Bourns College of Engineering have solved a problem that previously presented a serious hurdle for the use of graphene in electronic devices.</p> 
      <div id="attachment_17291" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 250px">
       <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/?attachment_id=17291" rel="attachment wp-att-17291"><img class="size-medium wp-image-17291 " alt="microscopic image of graphene" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/Graphene-Device-UCR-png-250x356.jpg" width="250" height="356" /></a>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">Scanning electron microscopy image of graphene device used in the study. The scale bar is one micrometer. The UCR logo next to it is implemented with etched graphene.</p>
      </div> 
      <p>Graphene is a single-atom thick carbon crystal with unique properties beneficial for electronics including extremely high electron mobility and phonon thermal conductivity. However, graphene does not have an energy band gap, which is a specific property of semiconductor materials that separate electrons from holes and allows a transistor implemented with a given material to be completely switched off.</p> 
      <p>A transistor implemented with graphene will be very fast but will suffer from leakage currents and power dissipation while in the off state because of the absence of the energy band gap. Efforts to induce a band-gap in graphene via quantum confinement or surface functionalization have not resulted in a breakthrough. That left scientists wondering whether graphene applications in electronic circuits for information processing were feasible.</p> 
      <p>The UC Riverside team – <a href="http://promise.ucr.edu/profile-technology-balandin.html">Alexander Balandin</a> and <a href="http://www.ee.ucr.edu/~rlake/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.ee.ucr.edu/~rlake/']);">Roger Lake</a>, both electrical engineering professors, Alexander Khitun, an adjunct professor of electrical engineering, and Guanxiong Liu and Sonia Ahsan, both of whom earned their Ph.Ds from UC Riverside while working on this research – has eliminated that doubt.</p> 
      <p>“Most researchers have tried to change graphene to make it more like conventional semiconductors for applications in logic circuits,” Balandin said. “This usually results in degradation of graphene properties. For example, attempts to induce an energy band gap commonly result in decreasing electron mobility while still not leading to sufficiently large band gap.”</p> 
      <div id="attachment_8861" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 130px">
       <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/?attachment_id=8861" rel="attachment wp-att-8861"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-8861" alt="Alexander Balandin" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/profile-Alex-Balandin-130x78.jpg" width="130" height="78" /></a>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">Alexander Balandin, a professor of Electrical Engineering</p>
      </div> 
      <p>“We decided to take alternative approach,” Balandin said. “Instead of trying to change graphene, we changed the way the information is processed in the circuits.”</p> 
      <p>The UCR team demonstrated that the negative differential resistance experimentally observed in graphene field-effect transistors allows for construction of viable non-Boolean computational architectures with the gap-less graphene. The negative differential resistance – observed under certain biasing schemes – is an intrinsic property of graphene resulting from its symmetric band structure. The advanced version of the paper with UCR findings can be accessed at <a href="http://arxiv.org/abs/1308.2931" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://arxiv.org/abs/1308.2931']);">http://arxiv.org/abs/1308.2931.</a></p> 
      <p>Modern digital logic, which is used in computers and cell phones, is based on Boolean algebra implemented in semiconductor switch-based circuits. It uses zeroes and ones for encoding and processing the information. However, the Boolean logic is not the only way to process information. The UC Riverside team proposed to use specific current-voltage characteristics of graphene for constructing the non-Boolean logic architecture, which utilizes the principles of the non-linear networks.</p> 
      <div id="attachment_17290" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 130px">
       <a href="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/?attachment_id=17290" rel="attachment wp-att-17290"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-17290" alt="headshot of Roger Lake" src="http://ucrtoday.ucr.edu/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/roger-lake-130x78.jpg" width="130" height="78" /></a>
       <p class="wp-caption-text">Roger Lake, a professor of electrical engineering</p>
      </div> 
      <p>The graphene transistors for this study were built and tested by Liu at Balandin’s <a href="http://ndl.ee.ucr.edu/" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://ndl.ee.ucr.edu/']);">Nano-Device Laboratory</a> at UC Riverside. The physical processes leading to unusual electrical characteristics were simulated using atomistic models by Ahsan, who was working under Lake. Khitun provided expertise on non-Boolean logic architectures.</p> 
      <p>The atomistic modeling conducted in Lake’s group shows that the negative differential resistance appears not only in microscopic-size graphene devices but also at the nanometer-scale, which would allow for fabrication of extremely small and low power circuits.</p> 
      <p>The proposed approach for graphene circuits presents a conceptual change in graphene research and indicates an alternative route for graphene’s applications in information processing according to the UC Riverside team.</p>
