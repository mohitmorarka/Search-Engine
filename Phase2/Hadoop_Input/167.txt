       <h2>KIMBERLY HAMMOND</h2> 
       <p><strong> Professor of Biology<br /> Associate Dean for Recruitment and Outreach in the Graduate Division<br /> Office: 3314 Spieth Hall<br /> Phone: (951) 827-4767</strong><br /> <br /> E-mail: <a href="mailto:kimberly.hammond@ucr.edu">kimberly.hammond@ucr.edu</a></p> 
       <b></b>
       <p><b>Degree:</b> Ph.D., Colorado State University, 1989 </p> 
       <p>My research interests are in the area of animal physiological ecology and evolutionary physiology.&nbsp; More specifically, I am interested in how anatomical and physiological capacities meet environmental demands.&nbsp; For instance: when an animal is confronted by a greater energetic or physiological demand (cold temperatures, hypoxic conditions) can it compensate for that demand by increasing (or decreasing) physiological processing capacity?</p> 
       <p>Animals often meet changes in demand with changes in the size of organs and organ capacity. &nbsp; I am most interested in learning how these load/capacity relationships are reflected in an animal's life history.&nbsp; This approach demands an appreciation of both mechanistic physiology and ecology, and requires both field and laboratory research.&nbsp; In addition, I study animals at all stages of development, concentrating on the effects of environmental demands <i>in utero</i> and during adulthood.&nbsp; At present I work primarily in desert and montane systems, using rodents as study species.</p>
       <dir> 
        <p>Recent and current projects of my students, my colleagues, and myself:</p>
       </dir> 
       <ul> 
        <li>The interplay between physiological acclimation and genetic adaptation to hypoxia in deer mice.&nbsp; </li> 
        <li>Limits to metabolic energy output elicited by lactation, cold exposure, and exercise in laboratory mice, house mice and deer mice.&nbsp; </li> 
        <li>Effects of sub-lethal parasites on host physiology during lactation, cold exposure and food restriction in mice.&nbsp; </li> 
        <li>The correlation between organ size and aerobic performance in junglefowl </li> 
        <li>Changes in organ size and function in deer mice <em>(Peromyscus maniculatus)</em> along an altitudinal gradient.&nbsp; </li>
       </ul>
